A Love Letter to London: From Ind Coope and Charrington to The Kernel and Beavertown

London taught the world how to brew beer, then lost most of its great breweries, then rebuilt an entirely new beer culture out of a row of railway arches in Bermondsey. Three cities in one.

San Diego built its craft capital brewery-first. Melbourne built its bar-first. London did neither, and both, and something else — because London already had the breweries, then didn't, and the scene that came back had to invent itself from almost nothing.

San Diego grew up.
Melbourne curated.
London came back.

Third in a series: this essay completes the trilogy with our San Diego love letter and our Melbourne love letter. Three routes to the same question: what makes a great beer city?

The London that disappeared

Before the craft revival, London was a beer capital most modern drinkers never knew existed. Through the 19th and early 20th centuries the city was genuinely a brewing power — Charrington's at Mile End, Ind Coope out east and later in Romford, Watney Combe Reid in Pimlico, Whitbread on Chiswell Street, Truman's on Brick Lane, Courage on the south bank. These were not small operations. They were among the largest breweries in the world.

By 1971 many were already struggling, and Des de Moor's careful survey of London brewers in that year reads, in retrospect, like a list of names about to disappear. Mergers, closures, and the rising tide of national-distribution macro lager took most of them out through the 1970s and 1980s. Redevelopment of valuable inner-London industrial land finished the job. A city that once brewed the world's beer was left, by the late 1990s, with Fuller's in Chiswick, Young's (soon to move production out of Wandsworth), and a handful of family operations holding the line.

This is the piece most visitors miss. London's modern craft scene is not a continuation of the old. It is a restart.

How India Pale Ale came out of London

Before the collapse, there was the invention.

In the late 18th and early 19th centuries London Pale Ales were already being exported to British India. The journey was brutal — months on the open sea, round the Cape of Good Hope, through the equatorial heat — and ordinary beer did not survive it. Brewers adapted: higher strength, heavier hopping. Hops and alcohol are both natural preservatives. What came off the boat in Calcutta and Madras was a beer engineered for the voyage.

Bow Brewery (Hodgson's) in East London is the name most often credited with the early export trade. Burton-upon-Trent later became the dominant production centre because the mineral-rich Burton water suited the style, and names like Bass and Ind Coope's Burton operation turned IPA into a mass export. But the origin-story shape — a London beer style defined by trade geography, by the distance it had to travel — belongs to the city on the Thames.

Every IPA pint poured today, in any country, on any continent, traces back to a cargo manifest leaving the Port of London.

The long gap

From roughly 1980 to 2008, London was a bad beer city. Not for lack of drinking — the pub was still the pub — but for lack of choice. Most mainstream pubs poured four or five generic lagers, a bitter, a Guinness, and little else. Real-ale pubs existed, often excellent, but they were a subculture rather than a cultural default. Imported American craft was rare and expensive. The city that once exported beer to India was importing mass-market Pilsner from Europe.

The real-ale campaign (CAMRA) kept cask ale alive through these years, and that mattered — it meant London retained a living tradition of hand-pull, conditioning, and pub-served beer that most craft scenes have no native equivalent for. Fuller's London Pride never went away. Timothy Taylor's Landlord stayed on pub bars. But the sense that London was making anything new at the top of the scene had gone quiet.

The restart

Then, between 2009 and 2011, two breweries opened within two years of each other and quietly changed everything.

2009

The Kernel, Bermondsey

Evin O'Riordain set up a brewery in a railway arch under London Bridge, started making hop-forward Pale Ales and heavily-numbered IPA batches, and built the reference point for what modern London craft beer would sound like. The Kernel's labels — typewriter font, black and white, batch-numbered — and its commitment to style-specific brewing (a new hop, a new grist, a new beer) gave London craft a visual and editorial language it had lacked. The Bermondsey Beer Mile grew outwards from that one arch.

2011

Beavertown, North London

Logan Plant opened Beavertown in the basement of a Duke's Brew & Que restaurant in De Beauvoir Town. Where The Kernel was quiet and editorial, Beavertown was loud, colourful, and aggressively designed. Gamma Ray and Neck Oil went national. The packaging turned craft beer from specialist niche into a consumer brand that a non-specialist drinker would recognise on a shelf and pick up. Beavertown did for London what Stone had done for San Diego fifteen years earlier — made craft legible.

2012 — 2016

The wave

Once the pattern was set, the rest arrived fast. Partizan in Bermondsey (2012), Fourpure and Brew By Numbers in the same corner (2013), Pressure Drop and Five Points in Hackney (2013), Hackney Brewery, Wild Card in Walthamstow, Howling Hops. North London got Redchurch, Hammerton, Beerblefish. South got Canopy and By The Horns. By 2016 London had more than 100 active breweries again — not the scale of 1900, but the first time in decades that the number had gone up rather than down.

2017 — now

The correction

The scene has since been through its inevitable shakeout. Beavertown sold to Heineken in 2018, which split the community sharply. Several early-wave breweries closed or consolidated. Distribution tightened. But the infrastructure held. London today has a stable, varied, cask-and-keg craft scene that looks nothing like the post-collapse 1990s — a mature ecosystem rather than a gold rush.

The geography

London's craft-beer map has four centres of gravity.

South 1 — Bermondsey Beer Mile

The arches

The single most important craft-beer geography in the UK. A roughly one-mile stretch of railway arches between London Bridge and South Bermondsey, home to The Kernel, Partizan, Anspach & Hobday, Brew By Numbers, Moor Beer (London), Southwark Brewing, and others. Most open their taprooms Fridays and Saturdays, which turned a weekend in Bermondsey into one of the defining rituals of London craft.

Go: The Kernel, Partizan, Anspach & Hobday, Brew By Numbers, Moor Beer (London).
North 1 — Hackney & the north-east

The modern heartland

Hackney, Tottenham, and Walthamstow absorbed the second wave. Five Points, Pressure Drop, Howling Hops, Hammerton, Beavertown (before the Heineken deal), Wild Card — clustered breweries with taprooms, food, rotating guest lines. If Bermondsey is where the movement began, Hackney is where it grew up.

Go: Five Points, Pressure Drop, Howling Hops, Hammerton, Wild Card.
Central — Specialist bars and bottle shops

Where the curation lives

London's central zones don't brew at scale, but they curate at scale. The Rake next to Borough Market — tiny, 100+ bottles, strict editorial standard — has been the craft-beer bar reference since the mid-2000s. The Southampton Arms in Gospel Oak, Cask Pub in Pimlico, the Euston Tap, Mother Kelly's in Bethnal Green, and the BrewDog Camden and Soho outposts pour rotating lists that feel curated rather than random.

Go: The Rake, The Southampton Arms, Cask Pub & Kitchen, Euston Tap, Mother Kelly's.
West — Cask heritage

What never went away

Fuller's in Chiswick is still brewing, still pouring, still running the pub estate that kept good beer on a bar through the gap years. Cask ale is a living tradition in London — not a re-enactment — and that is a gift most craft scenes don't have. The specialist cask pubs of west London pour Timothy Taylor's Landlord, Fuller's London Pride, and a rotation of guest bitters alongside whatever keg-craft the modern scene has sent over.

Go: The Dove (Hammersmith), The Harp (Covent Garden, cask reference), Sambrook's Brewery.

What makes a London beer bar a London beer bar

A few recurring traits across the best rooms:

  • Keg and cask on the same wall. A decent London bar pours both without apology. If you can't get a hand-pulled bitter next to a hazy IPA, the curation is incomplete.
  • A bottle fridge that does work. The kegs turn over, but the fridge is where the range lives. A good London bar has 40–100 bottles and cans that nobody else is pouring that week.
  • Pub DNA. Unlike San Diego taprooms, which descend from the brewery, London beer bars descend from the pub. The room is older. The building is often older than the scene. That inheritance is part of the appeal.
  • Weekend-only taprooms. Bermondsey and Hackney both built a culture where the brewery taproom is open Friday evening and Saturday afternoon. It creates a rhythm that matches how people actually want to drink in a big city.

The honest critique

London's shadow side is scale and price. Rent pressure on arch and warehouse space means the margin for a small brewery is brutal. Distribution concentration — a handful of large groups acquiring the old-independent names — has thinned the sense of a community scene that existed in 2013. And the pub estate, while still the deepest in Europe, is under enough economic strain that every year some very good beer rooms close.

The answer is the same as in the other two cities: use the scene that actually exists, not the one you remember. London in 2026 is less wild than it was in 2014, but it is more stable. The great rooms are still great. The weekend in Bermondsey still works. Fuller's London Pride is still pulled in Chiswick. The scene is no longer rebuilding. It is running.

London didn't inherit its craft scene.
It had to build one.

A two-day visitor itinerary

If you have a weekend:

  • Saturday afternoon — Bermondsey. Start at The Kernel, work east through Partizan, Anspach & Hobday, and Brew By Numbers. Don't try to do all of them; pace matters. Finish at Moor Beer for a proper lunch-beer pairing.
  • Saturday evening — central. The Rake for tight curation, then The Harp for a cask flight, then wherever the last pint of the night feels right.
  • Sunday afternoon — Hackney. Five Points taproom, Pressure Drop, Howling Hops tank bar. Different energy to Bermondsey — younger rooms, more food, more of a Saturday-night-in-Sunday-afternoon feel.
  • Sunday evening — a proper pub. The Southampton Arms, the Euston Tap, or any good west-London cask room. Close the weekend the way London has always closed weekends.

Why this matters for PINtPOINT

London is the hardest of the three cities to navigate on intuition alone.

San Diego is a brewery-first city. You can look at the map, pick the names, and build a decent evening from brand recognition.

Melbourne is a bar-first city. You can follow a good bar and trust what's on the wall.

London is both — brewery taprooms, specialist bars, historic pubs, cask rooms, bottle shops, all pouring rotating lines across the biggest beer city in Europe. On any given Saturday, the question of where to go has more plausible answers in London than in San Diego and Melbourne combined. The live-tap decision is not a luxury. It is the only way to make the city legible.

A final word

The strangest thing about London's beer story is that it is almost young. The pubs are old, the cask tradition is old, and IPA itself is older than most living breweries. But the scene you drink in today — the arches, the keg lines, the specialist bars, the weekly brewery taprooms — is barely fifteen years old.

It disappeared once. It can be walked, drunk, and enjoyed now because a handful of brewers decided the gap could be closed. They were right.

In San Diego, you remember the breweries.
In Melbourne, you remember the bars.
In London, you remember that it came back.

Frequently asked questions

Who started London's craft-beer revival?

The modern revival is dated to 2009–2011. The Kernel Brewery opened in a Bermondsey railway arch in 2009, setting the reference point for independent hop-forward London brewing. Beavertown opened in a North London restaurant basement in 2011, bringing packaging design and national distribution that made craft beer legible to non-specialist drinkers. Together they set the pattern the rest of the scene followed.

What happened to the big London breweries like Ind Coope and Charrington?

Most of the historic big-six London brewers — Charrington, Ind Coope, Watney Combe Reid, Whitbread, Truman's and Courage — closed, merged, or moved production out of London during the 20th-century consolidation. Redevelopment of valuable inner-London industrial land and the rise of national-distribution macro lager finished the job. By the late 1990s only Fuller's, Young's and a handful of family operations remained. The modern craft scene rebuilt from scratch rather than inheriting the old.

Where does India Pale Ale come from, and did it really come from London?

IPA evolved from late-18th and early-19th century London Pale Ales brewed for export to British India. The long sea voyage demanded higher strength and heavier hopping to preserve the beer. Bow Brewery (Hodgson's) in East London is most often credited with the early export trade. Burton-upon-Trent later became the dominant IPA production centre because its mineral-rich water suited the style, but the origin-story shape of IPA belongs to London's trade geography.

What is the Bermondsey Beer Mile?

A cluster of independent craft breweries operating out of railway arches along a roughly one-mile stretch between London Bridge and South Bermondsey. The Kernel anchored it in 2009. Partizan, Anspach & Hobday, Southwark Brewing, Brew By Numbers, Moor Beer and others followed. Many open their taprooms only on Fridays and Saturdays, which turned Saturday afternoons in Bermondsey into a defining ritual of London craft beer.

What is distinctive about London's craft beer today?

Range, bar culture, and continuity with cask. London supports hop-forward Pale Ales and IPAs, modern lager, sour and mixed-fermentation, and a still-vital cask ale tradition — often on the same wall. It also has an unusually dense network of specialist craft-beer bars and pub-bottle-shops that curate rotating lists. And unlike many modern craft scenes, London never fully abandoned cask, so hand-pull and keg share a bar without friction.

San Diego gave the world West Coast IPA. Melbourne gave the world the craft-beer bar. London gave the world IPA itself, then gave it back a century later, then had to rebuild a city's beer culture out of railway arches when everything in between had quietly been lost.

Three love letters, three answers to the same question. Worth drinking in all three.
