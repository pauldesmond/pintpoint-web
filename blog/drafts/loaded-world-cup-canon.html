Loaded's X World Cup Canon: From Crisps to Meat, 1994–2001

The 1995 Lager'lympics was not a one-off. It was one entry in a recurring Loaded magazine format that started with the Crisps World Cup in June 1994 — the magazine's second issue, the format's first run — and was still going seven years later when The League of Gentlemen's Hilary Briss presided over the Meat World Cup in January 2001.

This page is the canonical index: nine known editions, with bylines, winners, and the through-lines — Antony Medley as franchise photographer across at least three of them, Phil Robinson writing the hard-liquor pieces, Rowan Chernin writing the lurid-sensory ones, and a sick-bin gag that recurs from the Vodka World Cup to the Booze World Cup four years apart.

The origin: Crisps, June 1994

The format started in Loaded's second issue. The story is told in James Brown's memoir Animal House, by Brown himself — he was the magazine's founding editor — and runs as follows. Sitting in the office, the publisher Alan Lewis (formerly editor of Sounds and the inventor of Kerrang!, by then a pub landlord in Berkshire) declared that Kettle Chips were superior to Walkers. Brown decided to settle it editorially.

"Sixty-four different flavours of crisps, which was actually very hard to do in those days as there weren't any local Sainsbury's or Tescos, and we had to throw in a few things like apple rings and corn-based snacks, but they were knocked out early on."— James Brown, Animal House

The semis were Walkers Cream Cheese & Chive against Walkers Beef & Onion, and Pringles Lights against McCoy's Chilli. The final was a draw — by the late rounds the panel could no longer taste the difference, so they introduced a "sophisticated scoring system" with columns for shape, crunch, sound, dinner-substitute compatibility, and hedgehog factor. When that also failed to break the tie, they phoned the Vatican.

"We put the phone on speaker and an Irish priest there who answered listened to what we were doing and replied, 'I think His Holiness is a Pringles man.'"— James Brown, Animal House

The actual verdict came via a different higher power. They phoned cab ranks in the towns where the finalists were made and asked the fare to the nearest airport. The cheapest won.

"That's science for you."— James Brown, Animal House

Brown's memoir is explicit that this was the format's seed: "The format became a loaded staple, then a magazine staple, and then twenty-five years on in the age of social media, TV host Richard Osman started using it on Twitter as if no one had ever done it before." Eight further editions in our canon below; more in the archive yet to be transcribed.

The canon, in chronological order

Edition 01Loaded #2 · June 1994

The Crisps World Cup

Story: James Brown (recounted in his memoir Animal House). Original feature uncredited in-page.

The format's origin. 64 entrants — padded with apple rings and corn snacks to make the number — running on a single page and spread inside the second-ever issue of the magazine. Final-round scoring grid covers shape, crunch, sound, dinner-substitute compatibility and "hedgehog factor". Final decided by the cheapest cab fare to the nearest airport, after a phone call to the Vatican failed to break the tie.

WinnerPringles (final: Walkers Cream Cheese & Chive v McCoy's Chilli ended drawn; Pringles via cab-fare arithmetic and Vatican blessing)
Edition 02Loaded #12 · April 1995 (supplement)

The Lager'lympics

Published in What's For Tea Love?, the magazine's first food-and-drink supplement.

62 lagers (framed in the editorial as 64 — Loaded kept the founding magic number even when the field was thin). Six rounds, drunk-panel adjudication, scoring across design / taste / drunk. Final 178–98. Documented in full on our dedicated reference page; this is the one the magazine's own archive doesn't carry.

WinnerBudvar (Budweiser Budvar, Czech Republic; beat Molson 178–98 in the final)

Edition 03Early Loaded, page 24–25 · issue TBC

The Vodka World Cup

Story: uncredited in-page. Judges nicknamed in-house as "Captain Mainwaring" and "Pike" — office personae, not the Dad's Army cast (Arthur Lowe had been dead since 1982).

Bracket-format vodka tasting that goes off the rails almost immediately. Tanqueray, which is gin, is accidentally voted into the vodka final because Filthy — the staffer pouring — is too drunk to keep the cups straight. The eventual real winner sees the sign-off swerve into Cold War piss-take: "It is Loaded dad's Amy Vodka World Cup. That's a lot of vodka. And I'll tell you one thing for nothing — the Russians can have it back. One. Two. Three. Altogether now: 'Who do you think you are kidding, Mr Yeltsin…'" The "Ein Berliner" sick-bin makes its first known appearance; it returns four years later in the Booze WC.

WinnerStolichnaya (Tanqueray reached the final by judging error)
Edition 04Loaded · February 1997

The Toast Olympics

Story: Phil Robinson.

Known from the archive but not yet transcribed from a clean scan. Phil Robinson's first known X World Cup byline. Per the convention of the format, expect a bracketed knockout of bread types or topping styles with predictable physical decline of the panel.

Edition 05Loaded · August 1997

The Ice Lolly World Cup

Story: Rowan Chernin. Subtitle: "loaded's quest for a real mother sucker".

By the second round the panel has lost the plot. Magnums "get scared and melt into streaky goo". The Mr Men refuse to leave their wrappers. Jason, the Irish production editor, takes an elastic band to the glasses and drops his Solero. Derek — the other office Irish bloke — declares the Bubble Gum Showtime "deliberately tastes of gonorrhoea". An open-window elastic-band war breaks out; Jon "Missing" Link, art editor, picks up a chair and smashes it through a closed window. "It's a lethal cocktail," shouts Mick, swinging a plastic bin. Krispy Ice is disqualified outright as the worst thing the panel has ever tasted.

Medal table1st Calippo · 2nd Bounty · 3rd Twister
Edition 06Loaded #51 · July 1998

The Booze World Cup

Story: Phil Robinson · photos: Antony Medley. Subtitle: "Don't feel the spirit, drink it".

Spirits-only knockout; the sick-bin is back, this time labelled "WORLD CUP SICK BIN — EIN BERLINER", the same gag from the Vodka WC four years earlier. The round names map the panel's physical decline: "You can't drink that much, you'll be sick" → "Let's get this finished" → "Everything is horrible, nothing is nice" → "I went to the toilet and I couldn't piss" → "My teeth itch — I'm going home". Iran, Morocco, Tunisia and Saudi Arabia are out at the first hurdle because "you can't run the Booze World Cup if you only drink Vimto" [sic]. The final pits Linie Aquavit against Beefeater London Distilled Dry Gin; both are declared to "taste like wank". The verdict comes by exhaustion, not preference.

WinnerBeefeater Gin (England; final v Linie Aquavit, Norway)
"We wanted David Niven in a bottle, what we've got is David Icke."— Booze World Cup, Loaded #51 · July 1998
Edition 07Loaded #72 · April 2000

The Stockings World Cup

Story: Rowan Chernin · photos: Stephen Perry. Subtitle: "32 qualifiers played over 64 legs, but only one winner".

Modelled openly on the antecedent: "First they invented sliced bread, then man walked on the moon, and then came the Bra World Cup. Then nothing else happened for a couple of months. And now … the Stockings World Cup." Knockout across 32 brands worn by models, judged in person. Wolford takes Sensuous in the final — the magazine catches the brand's design heritage neatly: "Stockings were originally a woman-covering device designed around the time of the Great Plague to keep the rickets free of fleas."

WinnerWolford (final v Sensuous)
Edition 08Loaded #74 · June 2000

The Art World Cup

Story: Johnny Cigarettes · photos: Antony Medley. Hosted by Tony Hart.

32 paintings, run as a knockout. The intro sets the production design exactly right: "Take 32 paintings, one art telly genius, a bunch of ignorant loaded berks and a big box full of cravats." Tony Hart — aged 75, decades into national-treasure status — is pictured holding the eventual winner. A "Charity Shop Dog Painting" wildcard reaches the quarter-finals before being beaten by Toulouse-Lautrec. The final pits Klimt against Degas. Klimt's The Three Ages of Woman (1905) is declared "now officially the best painting in the whole world".

WinnerGustav Klimt — The Three Ages of Woman (1905)
"Picasso hasn't captured angst. He's just captured a woman with a filling biting into an ice lolly."— Mick, Loaded panel, Art WC
Edition 09Loaded #79 · November 2000

The Sex Toy World Cup

Story: Will Storr · photos: Alan McFadden & Mark Kinge. Strapline: "Loaded Sex Awards".

Bracketed against £1,628.24 of merchandise from Soho. The format here is at its most catalogue-like — less drunk-panel write-up, more grid of items getting numerical scores — but the round-naming and seeding discipline is recognisably the same one James Brown set in 1994. Notable for being the only edition in the known canon where the journalistic angle is consumer-survey rather than tasting-panel.

Edition 10Loaded #81 · January 2001

The Meat World Cup

Story: Chris Burke · photos: Antony Medley. Presided over by Mark Gatiss in character as Hilary Briss, the sinister ginger butcher from The League of Gentlemen.

18 cuts of meat staged at a long table "like the disciples from the Last Supper", with a raised pig's head pride of place. The framing nods to creationism via butchery: "It all started, as many things did, in the Garden of Eden. 'Not apple again,' complained Adam to his other half. 'Sod that, I want my rib back.'" Hilary Briss arrives with some of "The Special Stuff" from his own Royston Vasey meat lockers. Sign-off line: "Pass the carving knife, mother."

The through-lines

Reading the canon end-to-end — rather than as discrete novelty pieces — reveals a small set of recurring elements that hold across nearly seven years and at least four different writers.

Antony Medley is the franchise photographer

Medley's byline appears on the Art WC, the Booze WC, and the Meat WC at minimum — three editions stretched across two and a half years. The visual register is consistent: judges photographed mid-decline, an over-laden table, a single hero shot of the winning object. He is to the Loaded World Cup what John Belushi was to Animal House: not the writer but the through-line.

Two writers carry the late canon

Phil Robinson writes the hard-liquor and savoury editions — Toast Olympics in 1997, Booze WC in 1998. Rowan Chernin writes the lurid-sensory ones — Ice Lolly WC in 1997, Stockings WC in 2000. Both authors handle the format's two principal failure modes: physical disgust and sensory overload.

The Sick Bin gag recurs

The "Ein Berliner" sick-bin first appears in the Vodka WC and shows up again, with the same Kennedy-mangling label, in the Booze WC four years later. It is the closest the format has to a recurring prop — a deliberate self-citation across editions for readers paying attention.

Celebrity hosts

The format gravitates toward high-low casting. Tony Hart hosts the Art WC. Mark Gatiss presides over the Meat WC in character as Hilary Briss. The Vodka WC features office staffers nicknamed Captain Mainwaring and Pike. The hosts are not Loaded's own; they are imported and bent to the format's purposes.

Format-by-derangement is the format

The verdict mechanism is rarely the published scoring system. Crisps was decided by airport cab fare. Vodka had a finalist installed by mis-pouring. Booze was conceded by exhaustion. Ice Lolly involved a chair through a window. The bracket is the alibi; the chaos is the content.

Why this matters

The X World Cup is now most visible in its descendants — quote-tweet bracket polls on Twitter, "Best Crisps" polls on TV panel shows, the entire Richard Osman corner of British media. Brown's memoir is direct on this: "twenty-five years on in the age of social media, TV host Richard Osman started using it on Twitter as if no one had ever done it before." That lineage gets repeatedly attributed sideways or forwards; this page is a record of where it actually started.

It also gives the Lager'lympics its proper editorial position. The 1995 Lager World Cup wasn't an isolated stunt that happened to crown Budvar; it was the second edition of a format the magazine already trusted, and it inherits the founding number (64 lagers framed even though the field was actually 62) directly from the Crisps WC the year before. Loaded kept the magic number even when the entrants thinned.

FAQ

How many "X World Cup" editions did Loaded run?

At least ten that we can name — nine confirmed from primary scans (Crisps, Lager'lympics, Vodka, Toast, Ice Lolly, Booze, Stockings, Art, Sex Toy, Meat) and several more that are referenced in passing in other features (Computer Games WC, Cricket WC, Bra WC) plus the Issue 100 "World Cup of World Cups" retrospective in 2003. PINtPOINT holds the full Loaded archive; gaps in this canon will be closed as further pages are transcribed.

Which was the first?

The Crisps World Cup, in Loaded's second issue (June 1994). The story is told by editor James Brown in his memoir Animal House. It started as a way to settle whether Kettle Chips were really better than Walkers — a claim that publisher Alan Lewis was making in the office — and ended with a phone call to the Vatican and a cost-comparison of cab fares to airports.

Who won the Crisps World Cup?

Pringles, but contingently. The final between Walkers Cream Cheese & Chive and McCoy's Chilli ended drawn under the panel's "sophisticated scoring system" (shape, crunch, sound, dinner-substitute compatibility, hedgehog factor). They phoned the Vatican; an Irish priest answered and declared "I think His Holiness is a Pringles man". They then phoned cab ranks in the manufacturing towns of the finalists and asked the fare to the nearest airport. The cheapest won.

Who hosted the Art World Cup?

Tony Hart, presenter of Hart Beat and Take Hart. He was 75 at the time of the Loaded Art World Cup (Issue 74, June 2000) and is pictured in the feature holding the eventual winner, Klimt's The Three Ages of Woman. Story by Johnny Cigarettes; photos by Antony Medley.

Who hosted the Meat World Cup?

Mark Gatiss, in character as Hilary Briss, the sinister ginger butcher from The League of Gentlemen. He brought "some of The Special Stuff" from his Royston Vasey meat lockers to the table. The feature ran in Loaded #81 (January 2001), written by Chris Burke with photos by Antony Medley.

What is the "Ein Berliner" sick bin?

A bin used by the Loaded panel to throw up into during tasting features, labelled with the Kennedy-mangling "WORLD CUP SICK BIN — EIN BERLINER" in marker pen. It appears in both the Vodka World Cup (early Loaded) and the Booze World Cup (Issue 51, July 1998), four years apart — the closest the format has to a recurring prop.
