## Three structural arguments (stacked)

The piece carries three lines of analysis simultaneously:

### 1. The 6-tier honesty spectrum

Revivals fall on a gradient from naked nostalgia retail to recipe-faithful continuity. The further from a marketing department, the more credible.

| Tier | Anchor | Example | Honesty |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Just the name | Watney's Party Seven (2021) | Pure nostalgia branding |
| 2 | "Updated recipe" | Carling Black Label (Mar 2026, +0.7% ABV); Belhaven Twisted Thistle (Jan 2026, –1.6% ABV) | Honest about commercial; dishonest about heritage |
| 3 | Archive ledger | Allsopp's Double Diamond (1922 inspiration + Citra/Columbus/Mosaic) | Heritage cosplay |
| 4 | Surviving bottle | Tennent's 1868 stout commemorative | Artefact-anchored |
| 5 | Scholar / homebrew | Ron Pattinson, Barclay Perkins blog, homebrewtalk.com, beersmith.com | DIY purity |
| 6a | Continuity by people | Burton Bridge DBA — ex-Ind Coope brewers picking up DBA after Carlsberg killed it (Mar 2015) | Humble continuity |
| 6b | Continuity by place | Jennings (Cockermouth) — Canfield purchase 2024/25, 100 archive recipes, cask-first | Place-based continuity |
| 6c | Continuity by region (corporate-licensed hybrid) | Boddingtons (JW Lees brewing under AB InBev licence, late 2025) — Manchester yeast non-negotiable | Hybrid: mega-corporate IP, regional execution |

### 2. The 2023 UK alcohol duty reform rewrites recipes

> *"Since August 2023, how drinks in the UK are taxed has changed. Instead of being based on the categories of wine, beer, spirits and ciders, alcoholic beverages are now taxed based on their strength. This means drinks with an ABV below 3.5 per cent are taxed at a lower rate."* — Daily Record

ABV manipulation table — both directions:

| Beer | Was | Now | Direction | Why |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Allsopp's Double Diamond | original ~6% | **3.8%** | DOWN | "Sessionable" + duty-friendly |
| Belhaven Twisted Thistle IPA | 5.6% | **4.0%** | DOWN | Duty / cost ("evolve our range") |
| Marble Beer flagship pale | 3.9% | 3.4% → **3.9%** (reverted) | DOWN then UP | Customers grumbled, brewery climbed back |
| Grolsch Premium Pilsner | 5.0% (orig) → 4.0% (2020) → **3.4%** | DOWN | 25-year slow dilution |
| Carling Black Label | original ~4% | **4.7%** | UP | Premium line-extension positioning |

Allsopp's confession in this context:
> *"Revived as a 3.8% pale ale that drinks like a lager"* — Jamie Allsopp's own launch text

That's a duty-band choice with aesthetic consequences. The original Double Diamond was a strong IPA. "Drinks like a lager" is honest about where the duty reform pushes heritage recipes.

### 3. The Carlsberg villain pattern

The same corporate parent that kills heritage brands repeatedly creates the conditions for independents to revive them.

- Carlsberg discontinued Ind Coope **Draught Burton Ale (DBA)** → Burton Bridge picked it up (Mar 2015)
- Carlsberg Marston's closed **Jennings Brewery** (Cockermouth) Nov 2022 → Canfields bought it back
- Carlsberg Marston's also recently axed **Banks's Mild** and **Bombardier**
- Carlsberg Marston's = the recurring antagonist

This is journalism-grade: *kill the brand → let it be rescued by enthusiasts who do it better*.

### 4. Heritage revival as gateway drug for cask beer

The CAMRA piece on the Boddingtons / JW Lees revival makes this argument explicitly. Heritage brands act as a low-risk entry point for younger drinkers who'd otherwise be intimidated by a row of unknown handpulls. The cask renaissance is being seeded by familiar names, not by craft education.

Evidence:
- **Bass Pale Ale** — +19% growth year-on-year (cited in the Boddingtons piece)
- **Boddingtons (JW Lees)** — Manchester venues already "not able to keep up with demand"
- The drinkers ordering it are *"young, fashionable, and about as far away from the cask beer stereotype as you can possibly get"*

> *"For many drinkers, especially younger ones, a row of handpulls featuring a range of products they've never heard of can be incredibly intimidating. In Boddingtons, a brand has been revived that people can easily trust."* — CAMRA What's Brewing, Oct 2025

But: the tension.
> *"This needs to be considered for what it really is: the world's largest brewer muscling in on bar space typically reserved for the UK's small, independent brewers."*

The same revival that draws new drinkers to cask also crowds smaller breweries off the handpulls. Two true things at once.

### 5. The shelf-presence defence (the editorial soft-nuance)

Paul's quote in his own words:
> *"The beer is nothing special, but I do love seeing them in supermarkets with original(ish) branding, with the little cartoon fellow from the adverts. It may not be my choice, but I'd rather see that on the shelves than Fosters and Budweiser."*

The piece can't be all cynicism. Cultural-visibility-of-heritage-brands has its own value even when the liquid is mediocre. Better a flawed Double Diamond next to John Bull's cartoon than another row of identical macro-lagers.

This softens the "revivals are hauntings" thesis into something truer: **be honest about what a revival is AND still want it on the shelf**.

## Cases catalogued

### Watney's Party Seven — Tier 1
- Revived January 2021 by Watney's (the 2016-revived brand co.)
- Pandemic-driven, home-drinking nostalgia
- No recipe-faithfulness claim
- 9 pints in a single can — *"It is a very satisfying experience to twist the nozzle and see and hear beer pouring into the glass"*
- Source: https://metalpackager.com/2021/01/metalpackager-com-watneys-party7/

### Allsopp's Double Diamond — Tier 3
- First brewed 1876 as an IPA. Discontinued 2003.
- Allsopp's Brewery (modern revival of 19th-c Burton brewer) relaunched Oct 2024
- 3.8% ABV. Citra, Columbus, Mosaic hops. "Inspired by 1922 release"
- James Allsopp found original Allsopp ledger of recipes
- Now in Tesco bottles (4 × 440ml, £5.75 / £5 Clubcard)
- Sources:
  - https://www.thedrinksbusiness.com/2024/10/iconic-70s-beer-makes-a-comeback/
  - https://www.hertsad.co.uk/news/24909782.beer-column-double-diamond-revived-allsopp/
  - https://www.dailyrecord.co.uk/lifestyle/tesco-brings-back-nostaglic-90s-35303513

Reader comments (gold):
> *"Double Diamond was never 'iconic', it was universally loathed. Personally, I thought it was a disgusting drink. Artificial, sickly sweet, full of caramel and sticky. If a few drops had run down the side of the glass you needed wash your hands."* — Richard Platts

> *"Piss off! Double Diamond was along with Watneys Red Barrel and 'a brew' called Kingpin the worst shite that us teenagers were only offered in the 1960's by heavy marketing campaigns!"* — Bill Barson

> *"I loved the Double Diamond 'old taste' before it was taken off the market. I don't think that this new version is going to be as good but I shall sample and see."* — Eric Wood

Famous slogans worth lifting: *"Double Diamond works wonders"* / *"You're only here for the beer"* / Piccadilly Circus neon / "the government considered nationalising it"

### Tennent's 1868 stout commemorative — Tier 4
- Bottle found by Jim Anderson (Geelong Skindivers Club) in 1970s
- From the wreck of *The Light of The Age*, sunk by drunken captain off Port Phillip Heads, Melbourne, 16 Jan 1868
- Returned to Wellpark Brewery, Glasgow, 150 years later (Nov 2018)
- "One of the oldest bottles of beer in Britain" — pre-dates Tennent's Lager (1885)
- Tennent's brewed commemorative stout from archive recipes inspired by the artefact
- Source: https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-scotland-glasgow-west-46221849

> *"Unlike the drunken captain who ran his ship aground close to Port Phillip Heads, Jim has ensured his historic cargo reached its final destination — by flying round the world to put it in place himself."* — Alan McGarrie, Tennent's group brand director

Irresistible detail: *the wreck was caused by drunkenness*. Beer preserved by a beer-related disaster.

### Carling Black Label — Tier 2 (line extension)
- Returns March 16 2026 — Molson Coors
- 4.7% ABV (vs Carling Original 4.0%)
- Brewed in Burton-on-Trent, 100% British barley, cold-filtered
- 4-packs and 10-packs of 568ml cans
- "Black Label" branding dropped in 1997, beer vanished from shelves shortly after
- Famous slogan: *"I bet he drinks Carling Black Label"*
- *"designed for today's beer market"* — Kevin Fawell, Molson Coors UK sales director — the Tier 2 honest framing
- Sources:
  - https://www.lincolnshirelive.co.uk/news/uk-world-news/iconic-70s-beer-returns-uk-10837127
  - https://www.thesun.co.uk/money/38271677/iconic-90-s-beer-returning-uk-supermarkets/

### Belhaven Twisted Thistle IPA — Tier 2 (ABV-downgrade)
- Original 5.6% ABV → relaunch **4.0% ABV** (a downgrade)
- Owned by Greene King (since 2005)
- Relaunched January 2026
- Suzie Carlaw (marketing): *"fresh new look and a refreshing taste… ongoing mission to evolve our range while celebrating all of the great things that Belhaven Brewery is known for"*
- Source: https://www.thesun.co.uk/money/37931026/twisted-thistle-beer-returns-new-recipe/

Reader comments (drinker policing in real time):
> *"Well OK but it's not only the look that is new — this beer used to be 5.6%."*
> *"Relaunch is a bit of a stretch, it's a completely different beer."*
> *"Relaunch? That didn't last long the first time around."*

### Burton Bridge DBA — Tier 6a (continuity by people)
- Original Ind Coope DBA, launched 1976, CAMRA Champion Beer of Britain
- Carlsberg discontinued it
- Social-media campaign demanded a brewer pick it up
- Burton Bridge directors *had worked on the original at Ind Coope*
- Debuted at Burton CAMRA Beer Festival March 2015
- 4.8% ABV, Style: "Historical Beer - Burton Ale"
- 1,399 ratings on Untappd, 3.51 average
- Source: https://untappd.com/b/burton-bridge-brewery-draught-burton-ale-dba/1024410

The defining quote — humble, honest, the opposite of marketing:
> *"It would be impossible to brew an exact replica, but we are sure it is as close as possible."* — Burton Bridge

Drinker reactions confirm continuity:
> *"smooth, sweet, a little bitter"* / *"absolutely gorgeous bitter, rich and almost sticky on the palate"* — Untappd users

### Boddingtons Bitter (JW Lees) — Tier 6c (corporate-licensed regional hybrid)
- Strangeways Brewery founded 1778, Boddington name from 1853, sold to Whitbread 1989, acquired by Interbrew (now AB InBev) 2000
- Recipe changed late 1980s/early 1990s — from straw-pale + sharp astringency (invert sugar + English whole-leaf hops) to browner and less bitter
- 2004: Strangeways closed. 2012: Hydes-brewed cask version discontinued. Then: nitrokeg + nitrocan only
- Late 2025: JW Lees announces at AGM it will brew cask Boddingtons under licence from AB InBev
- Launched at JW Lees' Founders Hall, Manchester
- **Brewed with JW Lees' OWN house yeast** — *"a 'non-negotiable' part of the licence agreement with AB InBev"* (Michael Lees-Jones, production director)
- "Soft, bready character, reminiscent of fresh dough" — author of CAMRA piece
- Timing: end of Oasis world tour 2025; Manchester at cultural peak
- Source: https://wb.camra.org.uk/2025/10/10/boddingtons-is-back

Honest critic verdict from the CAMRA piece (the whole article is worth lifting from):
> *"this beer is not reinventing the wheel — there are far more interesting and flavourful pints available, even from JW Lees itself. But I consider the resurgence of Boddingtons is about more than flavour."*

The hybrid model — corporate IP + regional brewing + non-negotiable local yeast — is a NEW pattern. It splits the difference between Tier 2 (corporate marketing revival) and Tier 6 (continuity). The brand is corporate; the execution is regional; the authenticity claim rests on the Manchester yeast culture rather than the original recipe (which is gone — the post-1990s changes broke that line).

The "Cream of Manchester" / Melanie Sykes / *"By 'eck it's gorgeous"* / Friends reference / Madchester era — the brand's cultural saturation is what gives this revival its gravity, more than the liquid does.

### Jennings Brewery — Tier 6b (continuity by place)
- Closed Nov 2022 by Carlsberg Marston's Brewing Company
- Bought by Kurt & Rebecca Canfield (local couple, Cockermouth)
- New MD Chris France — *"recipes for about 100 beers"*
- Cumberland Ale + Bitter coming back cask-first — *"the best format for our beer and the most traditional format"*
- Sneck Lifter and Cocker Hoop *"overriding favourites to come back"*
- Bottled-by-Carlsberg interim until March 2025, then brand rights transfer
- CAMRA's Stephen Walker: *"I hope they won't just live in the past, that they'll progress and do new stuff"*
- Source: https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cr46p61vxe3o

## Sister project — Romford Universe

(Standalone, not part of this PINtPRESS piece. Captured here for cross-linking.)

The Romford Brewery (Ind Coope Romford):
- Founded 1708 (Benjamin Wilson)
- Ind / Coope acquisitions 1799 / 1845
- Peak ~1,000 employees, 20 acres
- Closed 1993 by Allied Lyons
- Site → "The Brewery" shopping complex (opened 2001)
- Historic elements survive: original buildings = Havering Museum, ornate iron gates, copper brewing vat, obelisk
- Main beers: John Bull Bitter, Long Life, Romford Brewers Bitter (1980s)
- None revived

The 1983 Pilgrimage booklet (Havering Museum, Object of the Month July 2015):
- "Romford Brewers Bitter Pilgrimage" challenge
- 24 tied pubs, 6 May – 30 July 1983
- Prizes tiered (dart flights at 6 pubs, VIP brewery tour at 24)
- Booklet includes sketches + descriptions of all 24 pubs
- *"some of which are sadly no longer operating today"*

The shape match to the Firkin Universe:
- Defined closed-brewery (Ind Coope Romford vs Bruce/Firkin)
- Documented passport challenge (1983 booklet vs 1990 Firkin leaflet)
- 24 mapped tied-houses with living/lost split
- Predates the Firkin Ale Trail by 7 years — Bruce's leaflet stood on Romford's shoulders

Sources:
- https://www.londonshoes.blog/2020/05/28/the-romford-brewery-its-history-legacy/
- https://www.haveringmuseum.org.uk/object-of-the-month-july-2015/
