England 2-1 Germany · The Beer World Cup XI Final

Beer World Cup XI · Final · 23 June 2026
🏴󠁧󠁢󠁥󠁮󠁧󠁿 England 2 – 1 Germany 🇩🇪
Sixty years on from '66 · Pub VAR confirmed · Hop Back winner
EnglandDraught Bass (14') · Hop Back Summer Lightning (87')
GermanyAugustiner Helles (71')

In 1994, Loaded launched into a summer when America was hosting the World Cup and England had failed to qualify. Thirty-two years later, the World Cup is back in North America, England are still carrying 1966 around like a family heirloom, and somehow the national drink has staged the comeback football never quite manages: cask bitter has won the Beer World Cup.

Sixty years after England beat Germany at Wembley, the beer version has gone one better. Not with lager. Not with craft haze. With warm, hand-pulled, cellar-dependent, impossible-to-export cask ale.

A Hop Back Summer Lightning winner three minutes from full time settled a final that had no business being this close.

It was as close as anyone had predicted. Augustiner Munich, the 1328-founded Bavarian captain whose Helles has been served continuously for nearly seven hundred years, had been the bookies' favourite all tournament. England's Cask XI, frequently described by neutrals as warm, flat, and somebody's nan's beer, had arrived in the final the way they had arrived in every previous round: by knowing exactly which beer to bring on, and when.

The Pub VAR booth confirms the score. England 2, Germany 1. The trophy stays in the cellar.

The road to the final

England's path looked, on paper, generous. A group stage that took care of itself with the right players in the right positions; a round-of-sixteen tie won on cellar work; a quarter-final against Czech Republic that turned into a slow tactical victory for Timothy Taylor's Landlord, who outlasted Pilsner Urquell over a long second half. The semi-final against Belgium was the harder of the two — abbey lineage back to 1230, a starting eleven with Westvleteren 12 captaining and Rochefort 10 in midfield, and the kind of religious silence at half-time you only get when a beer takes itself seriously. Belgium scored first through Cantillon Fou' Foune (a lambic that does not normally play in midfield but had been picked there for its versatility). Bass equalised. Harvey's Sussex Best, on as a 60th-minute substitute, scored the winner.

Germany cruised through their side of the draw with the casual contempt of a side that has been brewing without interruption since the 14th century. Augustiner picked up two early group goals; Schneider Aventinus banged in a hat-trick against the Netherlands in the round of sixteen; Schlenkerla Märzen smoked his way past USA in the quarters. The semi-final against Czech Republic could have gone either way until Spaten Münchner Hell stepped up in extra time and reminded everybody who actually invented the modern lager schedule.

Pre-match

By kick-off the omens had stopped being subtle. Sixty years since Wembley. Thirty-two since Loaded, USA 94 and England watching from home. A German captain founded in 1328. An English side built from cellars, handpulls and mild national delusion. Pub VAR had seen enough.

The teams emerged from the tunnel to the kind of pandemonium you only get at a Pub VAR final. England, ever the gracious hosts, in their traditional cellar-cool whites — handpulls polished, cask-bands turned to the right pressure. West Germany in crisp Bavarian whites with black trim, doing those nervous little jogs that bottom-fermented lagers do when there is a referee somewhere. Augustiner Helles ambled out at his own speed, the way 1328-vintage captains do. The coolest man on the pitch was the youngest. The youngest beer on the pitch was Hop Back Summer Lightning. File that away.

First half

Germany kicked off. They always do. Augustiner Helles took possession in the centre circle and held it for what felt like the entire first ten minutes — a beer that does not so much attack as occupy. Schneider Aventinus had a half-chance from a long ball played in by Weihenstephaner Hefeweißbier; Tegernseer Hell flashed a wide shot from the edge of the area that the Pub VAR review confirmed was definitely refreshing but not on target.

England's plan was, as anyone who had watched their previous six matches could have predicted, to wait. To let Germany do most of the work. To let the cellar settle. And then, in the 14th minute, to score.

14'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 1-0 GERMANY (Draught Bass) — Well, this came out of nothing! A long diagonal ball from Fuller's London Pride finds Bass ludicrously unmarked on the edge of the box. Bass takes one touch — a slight Burton sulphurous turn — and slots it past the German keeper into the bottom corner. The German defence stands around pointing into the space Bass has just taken advantage of as if to say aw … c'mon. The Coopers Tavern goes berserk in absentia. The 1,000-pub revival nodded once and went back to its pint. Greene King who?

Germany responded with structure. Spaten and Paulaner took control of the midfield, knocking the ball back and forth with the air of two brewers who have shared a Munich postcode for four hundred years. Erdinger blootered a header that Timothy Taylor's Landlord tipped over. Schlenkerla Märzen smoked a free kick into the wall. The half ended 1-0 to England. The Pub VAR booth checked the line three times for offside on Bass's goal. The line was fine. The line is always fine.

Second half

Germany came out re-pressed. Ayinger Celebrator was brought on for Tegernseer Hell at the break, a more direct doppelbock substitution that gave Germany more weight in the air. The pressure built. England held. Hooky Bitter mopped up two breaking attacks; Marston's Pedigree took a yellow card for a tactical foul on Andechs Doppelbock Dunkel that was, by any reasonable standard, beautifully timed.

Pub VAR — 64th minuteAndechs Doppelbock Dunkel goes down in the area under contact from Marston's Pedigree. The on-field call: no penalty. Pub VAR consults the rulebook (the rulebook is, as ever, an old beer mat with handwriting on it). The verdict: no penalty. Doppelbocks of that strength can be expected to fall over at the end of a long second half. Play on.

Germany equalised seven minutes later. It had to be Augustiner.

71'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 1-1 GERMANY (Augustiner Helles) — Weihenstephaner Hefeweißbier swings in a corner from the left. Augustiner rises above the marker — seven hundred years of perfect lager rises faster than you would think — and powers a header past Landlord and into the roof of the net. The Bavarian end celebrates by ordering a second Maß. The English end orders another half. Wir kommen zurück, mutters somebody in the press box. Hearts in mouths for England.

For fifteen minutes the final settled into an uncomfortable equilibrium. England's three-cask attacking line of Tribute, Pedigree and Old Peculier could not find space; Germany's midfield kept the ball in zones that Spaten has been keeping the ball in since the 14th century. It looked, briefly, like extra time. It looked, briefly, like Augustiner.

Then England made the substitution.

The winning goal

Boddingtons (cask) had been industrious all match without threatening — the J.W. Lees contract-brewed version that the Whippet in EC2 has been keeping alive, working hard in midfield but never quite finding the angles. The change, in the 83rd minute, was to bring on Hop Back Summer Lightning in his place. A 1989 strong golden ale, fresh-faced, fast, with a hop profile that nobody who had been studying England's traditional line-up was expecting.

The goal, when it came, came from nothing. A throw-in halfway inside the German half. Adnams Ghost Ship picked it up. A short pass to Fuller's London Pride, who turned. A long, raking ball over the German back four. Summer Lightning, on the run.

87'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 2-1 GERMANY (Hop Back Summer Lightning) — THE GOAL THAT MAKES IT CERTAIN. Summer Lightning, on the run, looks like he's wading through treacle on the heavy, cut-up pitch but he makes it to the area and, a millisecond before Spaten completes a futile pursuit, lashes an unstoppable shot into the bottom corner. The Wiltshire end loses its mind. The Pub VAR booth confirms the goal in three seconds — no offside, no foul in the build-up, just a beer that has been waiting since 1989 for exactly this moment and has finally taken it. ENGLAND ARE THE WORLD CHAMPIONS!!!

Germany pushed for the equaliser. They always do. Paulaner Salvator hit the woodwork in the 91st minute with a fizzer that Landlord probably didn't have covered. Augustiner had one more header that Landlord, magnificently, palmed over. "That's it," said the Pub VAR commentator. "That. Is. It." The final whistle went. The Cask XI went down on the pitch as if they had been the underdogs all along. In a sense they had. England, just as Pub VAR said they would, had won the Beer World Cup.

The trophy

The Pub VAR Cup was presented to Fuller's London Pride as captain. London Pride lifted it above his head with the slightly embarrassed expression of a man who had not really expected this. Timothy Taylor's Landlord stood beside him grinning, having stopped more shots than any goalkeeper in tournament history. Draught Bass, in conversation afterwards, said only that he had been doing this since 1777 and that, sometimes, things took time.

Augustiner shook hands with the entire England squad with the dignity that comes from being the better team on the night and losing anyway. "Wir kommen zurück," he was heard to say into the Sky Sports microphone. We will come back. Of course they will. They have been coming back since 1328.

Notes from Pub VAR

Player of the match. A tough one. The case for Hop Back Summer Lightning is obvious — late winner, fresh legs, fairy-tale ending. The case for Fuller's London Pride is structural — captained the whole tournament without a wasted touch. The Pub VAR booth, after long deliberation and the application of independent judgement, awards the medal to Timothy Taylor's Landlord for vice-captaincy, goalkeeping heroics, and the longest unbroken stretch of clean sheets the goalkeeper position has ever seen in a Beer World Cup. Tough on Summer Lightning. Football's a hard game.

Manager of the tournament. The publican. Every publican. The cellar work that kept twelve different bitters in decent nick across a six-week tournament was the actual achievement here. Nobody hands out trophies for cellar work. They should.

Disappointment of the tournament. Belgium going out in the semis. Westvleteren had not been seen at a competitive event for years and the abbey had asked the committee, very politely, not to mention any of their players by name. The committee published the line-up anyway. The lesson, as ever, is that if you do not want to be talked about you should not be the second-best beer in the world.

Honourable mention. Czech Republic, who played the best lager football of any team and went home in the semi-finals only because Spaten remembered, in extra time, that it was Munich who actually finished the pilsner style and Plzeň had only started it. Pilsner Urquell took the defeat with the kind of grace that comes from inventing a style that the entire world now drinks.

Sixty years on

It is sixty years on from 1966. It is the same country hosting the World Cup that hosted Loaded's launch year in 1994. None of it was planned. All of it lined up anyway. The result was always going to be cask, because cask only exists properly through pubs, cellars, and place, and Britain is the only country with a national infrastructure willing to keep it.

Three ways to be old, three definitions of heritage, one trophy. Belgium have the oldest brewers. Germany have the oldest brewery. England have the oldest format. The trophy went where the format goes. Drink properly. Lift trophy. Roll credits.
