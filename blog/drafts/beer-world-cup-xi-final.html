The Beer World Cup XI Final: England 4-2 Belgium

Beer World Cup XI · Final · 19 July 2026
🏴󠁧󠁢󠁥󠁮󠁧󠁿 England 4 – 2 Belgium 🇧🇪
Thirty-six years on from Bologna · Pub VAR confirmed · Bass hat-trick
EnglandDraught Bass (18', 101', 120') · Adnams Broadside (78')
BelgiumRochefort 10 (12') · Orval (89')

In 1994, Loaded launched into a summer when America was hosting the World Cup and England had failed to qualify. Thirty-two years later, the World Cup is back in North America, England are still carrying 1966 around like a family heirloom — and this time the national drink has staged the comeback football keeps almost managing: cask bitter has won the Beer World Cup.

Thirty-six years after Italia '90, Bologna, and David Platt's 119th-minute volley to knock Belgium out of a World Cup round of sixteen, the beer version has one-upped him. Not with lager. Not with craft haze. With warm, hand-pulled, cellar-dependent, impossible-to-export cask ale, delivered by a 250-year-old Burton pale ale off the bench in extra time.

A Draught Bass hat-trick — including a 101st-minute ghost goal that the Pub VAR booth is still re-watching, and probably will be in 2086 — settled a final that had no business going to extra time at all. Belgium's Trappist wall, led by a captain who is technically not for sale, had the game in its hands twice. And could not quite close it.

It was as close as anyone had predicted. Belgium — captained by Westvleteren 12, marshalled by Rochefort 10 in midfield, finished off with a Cantillon front-runner and Orval as the wild-yeast wildcard — had been the bookies' favourite from the moment Germany's Bavarian XI packed up and left. English cask, frequently described by neutrals as warm, flat, and somebody's nan's beer, had arrived in the final the way they had arrived in every previous round: by knowing exactly which beer to bring on, and when.

The Pub VAR booth confirms the score. England 4, Belgium 2, after extra time. The trophy stays in the cellar.

The road to the final

England's path looked, on paper, generous. A group stage that took care of itself with the right players in the right positions; a round-of-sixteen tie against Mexico that ended 3-2 on cellar work — Modelo Especial scored twice, proper goals, no dispute, but Timothy Taylor's Landlord in the seventy-third and Harvey's Sussex Best in the eighty-eighth made the difference. A quarter-final against Norway that turned into a slow tactical victory once Fuller's London Pride's captaincy took hold — Nøgne Ø #100 and Lervig Lucky Jack had the middle third for forty minutes, then ran out of Nordic craft and had to face two hundred years of English cellar management. The semi-final against Argentina was the harder of the two: Quilmes Cristal grabbed an early lead, Patagonia Amber equalised against Broadside just before the hour, and it took a Marston's Pedigree free kick in the eighty-fourth to see England through 2-1. Cellar work, again.

Belgium cruised through their side of the draw with the quiet contempt of a squad captained by a beer that has for decades been available only from a hatch in the abbey wall on days it chose. The Trappist front six annihilated USA 4-1 in the round of sixteen — Westvleteren 12, Rochefort 10 and Orval had walked the century wall down inside twenty minutes; Sierra Nevada Pale Ale got a proud consolation late on. The quarter-final against Spain was tighter than the neutrals expected — Alhambra Reserva 1925 and La Pirata Black Block have kept every European abbey side honest all tournament — and settled 2-1 on Rochefort's second-half header, an abbey specialty. The semi-final against France went to extra time before Cantillon Fou' Foune scored the strangest set-piece goal of the tournament to spare Jenlain Ambrée another twelve years of ridicule. Belgium arrived in the final without anyone in the football press quite noticing they had.

Pre-match

By kick-off the omens had stopped being subtle. Thirty-six years since Bologna. Thirty-two since Loaded, USA 94 and England watching from home. A Belgian captain that the world's beer press had to promise not to name in the pre-match interview and then named anyway. An English side built from cellars, handpulls and mild national delusion. Pub VAR had seen enough.

The teams emerged from the tunnel to the kind of pandemonium you only get at a Pub VAR final. England, ever the gracious hosts, in their traditional cellar-cool whites — handpulls polished, cask-bands turned to the right pressure. Belgium in monastic browns and cream — Westvleteren 12 ambling out with the yellow cap of the 12 balanced on his head, Rochefort 10 already looking like he was thinking about something more important than football, Orval doing the loose-limbed stretches of a beer that has three yeasts in it and no clear plan for any of them. The oldest beer on the pitch was Draught Bass. File that away.

First half

Belgium kicked off. They always do at abbey pace. Saison Dupont held the ball in the centre circle for what felt like the first ten minutes — a farmhouse beer that plays midfield the way it ferments: slowly, with confidence, at whatever temperature suits it. Cantillon Fou' Foune drifted into space on the right and produced a half-chance that Timothy Taylor's Landlord smothered. Duvel flashed a shot from the edge of the area that the Pub VAR review confirmed was definitely strong but not on target.

And then, twelve minutes in, the pressure told.

12'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 0-1 BELGIUM (Rochefort 10) — Cantillon Fou' Foune drives a corner short to Saison Dupont. Dupont pauses, sells one, and clips it deep into the second six-yard-box zone. Rochefort 10 — 11.3% ABV, unbothered by air travel, unbothered by physics — meets it with the calm of a beer that has been brewed by the same monks since 1595 and hangs the header into the top corner. The Belgian end blesses itself. The English end orders another round, defensively. Eric Todd in the press box mutters something about Cistercians and centuries and where do we get one of those.

England responded the way you respond when you have just shipped a goal you should not have shipped. They got cross. Six minutes later they were level.

18'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 1-1 BELGIUM (Draught Bass) — Pedigree gets clipped on the edge of the box. Fuller's London Pride is back on his feet before the ref blows, no theatre about it, and clips the free kick into the area. Bass is somehow alone on the penalty spot. One touch — a slight Burton sulphurous turn — and the ball is in the bottom-left corner. The abbey keeper looks at his back four. The back four look at the floor. Nobody can quite remember whose man Bass was. Greene King who?

The rest of the half settled into hand-to-hand brewing. Chimay Bleue and Achel Bruin took control of the midfield, knocking the ball back and forth with the air of two abbeys that have shared a diocese since the twelfth century. Duvel blootered a header that Timothy Taylor's Landlord tipped over. Orval clipped a chip toward the top corner that St. Bernardus Abt 12 nearly connected with. The half ended 1-1. The Pub VAR booth checked the line three times for offside on Bass's equaliser. The line was fine. The line is always fine.

Second half

Belgium came out patient. They brought St. Bernardus Abt 12 up alongside Rochefort 10 in a more direct 3-5-2, giving them more weight in the air. The pressure built. England held. Hooky Bitter mopped up two breaking attacks; Marston's Pedigree took a yellow card for a tactical foul on Orval that was, by any reasonable standard, beautifully timed.

Pub VAR — 64th minuteOrval goes down in the area under contact from Marston's Pedigree. The on-field call: no penalty. Pub VAR consults the rulebook (the rulebook is, as ever, an old beer mat with handwriting on it). The verdict: no penalty. Wild-yeast beers of that carbonation can be expected to fall over at the end of a long second half. Play on.

The Belgian bench wanted three penalties from that one. None was given. Belgium kept coming anyway. England absorbed. The next goal, when it arrived, came from a corner that nobody on the Belgian back four had any business letting drop.

78'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 2-1 BELGIUM (Adnams Broadside) — Adnams Ghost Ship floats a corner from the right. Chimay Bleue heads clear, but only to Brakspear on the edge of the box. Brakspear stabs at it under pressure, and the shot dribbles weakly toward the left post. It should be a routine clearance. Instead Achel Bruin sclaffs it skyward, and as the ball drops back into the six-yard box, Broadside — the late-arriving back-post specialist his Trafalgar name should have warned them about — buries it. 12 MINUTES from a beer trophy nobody else can compete for.

England held. Belgium pushed. Twelve minutes became eleven, then ten, then five, then two. The clock crept toward 90. Pub VAR signalled four added minutes. Wembley, in its head, was already singing. And then, with thirty seconds left, the ball broke in the England area and somehow ended up at Orval's feet.

89'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 2-2 BELGIUM (Orval) — Duvel drives at Boddingtons down the left channel and slips the ball inside to Saison Dupont. Dupont — dry-hopped, farmhouse, allergic to anything predictable — chips a floated ball over the English back four toward the penalty spot. Orval, who runs on Brettanomyces and a second fermentation nobody outside the abbey fully understands, controls it with the outside of his boot, spins, and lashes an unrepeatable shot into the top left corner. Landlord takes half a step, decides against the rest of the step, and just stands and watches. There is no scouting report for what Orval does. There has never been a scouting report for what Orval does. Extra time.

Extra time

England were understandably deflated; Belgium seemed too monastic to be elated. Fuller's London Pride gathered the eleven in the centre circle. He has captained this side through every round of the tournament. He had two things to say. The first was about cellar work. The second was about Bologna.

Whatever was said worked. England came out for extra time with their hands on the game.

101'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 3-2 BELGIUM (Draught Bass) — OR DOES IT?!! From the centre circle, Fuller's London Pride threads a pass down the right for Adnams Ghost Ship, who is somehow still running. Ghost Ship arrives at the byline and cuts a low, looping ball back into the area. Bass collects it ten yards out, back to goal, brings it down with the outside of his boot, half-pivots, and lashes a swivelling shot off the underside of the bar. The ball drops vertically. It hits the line. It hits somewhere around the line — depending on which end you were drinking at. Brakspear, who has a tap-in if he wants it, has already turned away with both arms aloft, having decided the question for himself. The Belgians surround the linesman. The linesman surrounds the Pub VAR booth. The Pub VAR booth consults its rulebook (still a beer mat). The verdict: the ball was in. Goal stands. The argument will outlive every brewer on the pitch.

Belgium surrounded the linesman, the referee, the fourth official, the kit man, the man who delivers the beer to the ground, and the Pub VAR official's nan. None of it changed anything. The goal stood. They were going to lose this final the same way Belgium had lost in Bologna in 1990: to an English shot in extra time that everybody knew was coming and could not, in the moment, do anything about.

Duvel was the first to break clear afterwards, breaking down the inside left and shooting inches wide, a powerful shot that Landlord probably didn't have covered, but he'd handled the ball en route and play was pulled back. Hearts in mouths for England, then back in throats, then in glasses. The clock crept on. 110. 115. 119. And then.

120'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 4-2 BELGIUM (Draught Bass) — HAT-TRICK!!! ENGLAND ARE THE BEER WORLD CHAMPIONS!!! Adnams Ghost Ship — who has somehow not stopped running — drills one last raking pass down the left channel. Bass is alone behind the Belgian line. He moves like a 250-year-old beer being asked to do one more thing, which is to say: not quickly, but with the certainty of having done it before. A millisecond before Westvleteren 12 can close, Bass plants his standing foot and unloads the kind of shot that ends careers in the wrong direction. Top corner. He barely jogs the celebration; Brakspear sprints in to lift him off the ground. The Pub VAR commentator starts to say the thing everyone expects him to say, thinks better of it, and settles for: "That's it. That is very much it." ENGLAND, JUST AS PUB VAR SAID THEY WOULD, HAVE WON THE BEER WORLD CUP.

The Pub VAR pitch flooded with well-wishers. The Belgian end stood for a long time, applauding. Westvleteren 12 shook hands with every Englishman on the field. Rochefort 10 dropped to his knees with the dignity of an abbey beer that has been doing this since 1595 and was not going to let one bad referee ruin a season. The Cask XI went down on the pitch as if they had been the underdogs all along. In a sense they had.

The trophy

The Pub Trophy was presented to Fuller's London Pride as captain. London Pride lifted it above his head with the slightly embarrassed expression of a man who had not really expected this. Draught Bass — who had not so much carried the team as carried the match ball home — stood at his shoulder with the kind of low, satisfied grin that 250-year-old Burton pale ales reserve for hat-tricks in finals. Timothy Taylor's Landlord stood on the other side grinning, having stopped more shots than any goalkeeper in tournament history. In conversation afterwards, Bass said only that he had been doing this since 1777 and that, sometimes, things took time.

Westvleteren, asked for a comment, gave the standard Sint-Sixtus reply: "We would prefer not to be interviewed about this." Rochefort 10 shook hands with the entire England squad with the dignity that comes from being the better beer on the night, in some fair sense, and losing to a beer that had been brewed in Burton since Trafalgar was still in the future.

Notes from Pub VAR

Player of the match. Hat-trick in a final. The case for anyone other than Draught Bass ends at the sentence "the man scored three." The 18th-minute equaliser was the moment England settled into the game. The 101st-minute ghost goal was the moment England settled into history. The 120th-minute winner was the moment Belgium ran out of abbey. Bass takes the match ball home. He has been doing this for 250 years. It is, you suspect, the first match ball he has not had to share. Honourable mention to Timothy Taylor's Landlord, who kept five clean sheets in the previous six matches and only conceded twice in this one when whole monasteries were sliding in around him. Goalkeeper of the tournament without question.

Manager of the tournament. The publican. Every publican. The cellar work that kept twelve different bitters in decent nick across a six-week tournament was the actual achievement here. Nobody hands out trophies for cellar work. They should.

Disappointment of the tournament. Germany going out to Paraguay in the round of thirty-two. The Bavarian XI had arrived talking a bigger game than they turned out to have — Augustiner Helles, Weihenstephaner, Schneider Aventinus and Schlenkerla Märzen were fancied by most neutrals to reach at least the semi-finals — and instead met a Paraguayan Pilsen squad that turned out to be sharper, meaner, and more patient than the Reinheitsgebot side's rotation could handle. Seven hundred years of doing the same thing perfectly, undone by a national lager that knew exactly which end of the second half to arrive at. There will be a longer piece on this. The short version is that Bavaria brought tradition; Paraguay brought the temperature the beer needed to be at.

Honourable mention. USA, who played the best craft-lager football of any team and went home in the round of sixteen only because Belgium's Trappist wall arrived early. Brooklyn Lager took the defeat with the kind of grace that comes from being the youngest serious lager-brewing nation in the world and still going toe-to-toe with the oldest tradition on the continent. The 1994 hosts return home with a record richer than the football would tell you.

Thirty-six years on

It is thirty-six years on from Bologna. Italia '90, round of sixteen, extra time, David Platt volleys home in the 119th minute to knock Belgium out of a World Cup. Sixty years on from Wembley — a different opponent then, but the same instinct: England, in extra time, against a continental heavyweight, finding the goal that ends the argument. Bass matched Platt in the 120th, and added two more for good measure. Loaded launched in 1994. None of it was planned. All of it lined up anyway. The result was always going to be cask, because cask only exists properly through pubs, cellars, and place, and Britain is the only country with a national infrastructure willing to keep it.

Three ways to be old, three definitions of heritage, one trophy. Belgium have the oldest brewers on the planet. Bavaria — sitting at home tonight — have the oldest brewery. England have the oldest format. The trophy went where the format goes. Drink properly. Lift trophy. Roll credits.
