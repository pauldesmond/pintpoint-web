The Beer World Cup XI Final: England 4-2 Germany

Beer World Cup XI · Final · 23 June 2026
🏴󠁧󠁢󠁥󠁮󠁧󠁿 England 4 – 2 Germany 🇩🇪
Sixty years on from '66 · Pub VAR confirmed · Bass hat-trick
EnglandDraught Bass (18', 101', 120') · Adnams Broadside (78')
GermanyAugustiner Helles (12') · Spaten Münchner Hell (89')

In 1994, Loaded launched into a summer when America was hosting the World Cup and England had failed to qualify. Thirty-two years later, the World Cup is back in North America, England are still carrying 1966 around like a family heirloom, and somehow the national drink has staged the comeback football never quite manages: cask bitter has won the Beer World Cup.

Sixty years after England beat Germany at Wembley, the beer version has gone one better. Not with lager. Not with craft haze. With warm, hand-pulled, cellar-dependent, impossible-to-export cask ale.

A Draught Bass hat-trick in extra time — including a 101st-minute ghost goal that the Pub VAR booth is still re-watching, and probably will be in 2086 — settled a final that had no business going to extra time at all. The score, when it stopped, was the same one Wembley settled on in 1966.

It was as close as anyone had predicted. Augustiner Munich, the 1328-founded Bavarian captain whose Helles has been served continuously for nearly seven hundred years, had been the bookies' favourite all tournament. England's Cask XI, frequently described by neutrals as warm, flat, and somebody's nan's beer, had arrived in the final the way they had arrived in every previous round: by knowing exactly which beer to bring on, and when.

The Pub VAR booth confirms the score. England 4, Germany 2, after extra time. The trophy stays in the cellar.

The road to the final

England's path looked, on paper, generous. A group stage that took care of itself with the right players in the right positions; a round-of-sixteen tie won on cellar work; a quarter-final against Czech Republic that turned into a slow tactical victory for Timothy Taylor's Landlord, who outlasted Pilsner Urquell over a long second half. The semi-final against Belgium was the harder of the two — abbey lineage back to 1230, a starting eleven with Westvleteren 12 captaining and Rochefort 10 in midfield, and the kind of religious silence at half-time you only get when a beer takes itself seriously. Belgium scored first through Cantillon Fou' Foune (a lambic that does not normally play in midfield but had been picked there for its versatility). Bass equalised. Harvey's Sussex Best, on as a 60th-minute substitute, scored the winner.

Germany cruised through their side of the draw with the casual contempt of a side that has been brewing without interruption since the 14th century. Augustiner picked up two early group goals; Schneider Aventinus banged in a hat-trick against the Netherlands in the round of sixteen; Schlenkerla Märzen smoked his way past USA in the quarters. The semi-final against Czech Republic could have gone either way until Spaten Münchner Hell stepped up in extra time and reminded everybody who actually invented the modern lager schedule.

Pre-match

By kick-off the omens had stopped being subtle. Sixty years since Wembley. Thirty-two since Loaded, USA 94 and England watching from home. A German captain founded in 1328. An English side built from cellars, handpulls and mild national delusion. Pub VAR had seen enough.

The teams emerged from the tunnel to the kind of pandemonium you only get at a Pub VAR final. England, ever the gracious hosts, in their traditional cellar-cool whites — handpulls polished, cask-bands turned to the right pressure. West Germany in crisp Bavarian whites with black trim, doing those nervous little jogs that bottom-fermented lagers do when there is a referee somewhere. Augustiner Helles ambled out at his own speed, the way 1328-vintage captains do. The oldest beer on the pitch was Draught Bass. File that away.

First half

Germany kicked off. They always do. Augustiner Helles took possession in the centre circle and held it for what felt like the entire first ten minutes — a beer that does not so much attack as occupy. Schneider Aventinus had a half-chance from a long ball played in by Weihenstephaner Hefeweißbier; Tegernseer Hell flashed a wide shot from the edge of the area that the Pub VAR review confirmed was definitely refreshing but not on target.

And then, twelve minutes in, the pressure told.

12'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 0-1 GERMANY (Augustiner Helles) — Weihenstephaner Hefeweißbier delivers a low cross from the left. Schlenkerla Märzen flicks it on. Augustiner Helles, almost unmarked at the back post, controls it on his chest and rolls it past Landlord into the corner. Seven hundred years of practising the same finish does that. The German end roars. The English end orders another round, defensively. Eric Todd in the press box mutters something about lab coats and pipettes.

England responded the way you respond when you have just shipped a goal you should not have shipped. They got cross. Six minutes later they were level.

18'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 1-1 GERMANY (Draught Bass) — Pedigree gets clipped on the edge of the box. Fuller's London Pride is back on his feet before the ref blows, no theatre about it, and clips the free kick into the area. Bass is somehow alone on the penalty spot. One touch — a slight Burton sulphurous turn — and the ball is in the bottom-left corner. The German keeper looks at his back four. The back four look at the floor. Nobody can quite remember whose man Bass was. Greene King who?

The rest of the half settled into hand-to-hand brewing. Spaten and Paulaner took control of the midfield, knocking the ball back and forth with the air of two breweries that have shared a Munich postcode for four hundred years. Erdinger blootered a header that Timothy Taylor's Landlord tipped over. Schlenkerla Märzen smoked a free kick into the wall. The half ended 1-1. The Pub VAR booth checked the line three times for offside on Bass's equaliser. The line was fine. The line is always fine.

Second half

Germany came out re-pressed. Ayinger Celebrator was brought on for Tegernseer Hell at the break, a more direct doppelbock substitution that gave Germany more weight in the air. The pressure built. England held. Hooky Bitter mopped up two breaking attacks; Marston's Pedigree took a yellow card for a tactical foul on Andechs Doppelbock Dunkel that was, by any reasonable standard, beautifully timed.

Pub VAR — 64th minuteAndechs Doppelbock Dunkel goes down in the area under contact from Marston's Pedigree. The on-field call: no penalty. Pub VAR consults the rulebook (the rulebook is, as ever, an old beer mat with handwriting on it). The verdict: no penalty. Doppelbocks of that strength can be expected to fall over at the end of a long second half. Play on.

The Bavarian end wanted three penalties from that one. None was given. Germany kept coming anyway. England absorbed. The next goal, when it arrived, came from a corner that nobody on the German back four had any business letting drop.

78'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 2-1 GERMANY (Adnams Broadside) — Adnams Ghost Ship floats a corner from the right. Schulz heads clear, but only to Brakspear on the edge of the box. Brakspear stabs at it under pressure, and the shot dribbles weakly toward the left post. It should be a routine clearance. Instead Hofbräu Original sclaffs it skyward, and as the ball drops back into the six-yard box, Broadside — the late-arriving back-post specialist his Trafalgar name should have warned them about — buries it. 12 MINUTES from a beer trophy nobody else can compete for.

England held. Germany pushed. Twelve minutes became eleven, then ten, then five, then two. The clock crept toward 90. Pub VAR signalled four added minutes. Wembley, in its head, was already singing. And then, with thirty seconds left, the ball broke in the England area and somehow ended up at Spaten Münchner Hell's feet.

89'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 2-2 GERMANY (Spaten Münchner Hell) — Schlenkerla hammers a free kick into a wall that fragments rather than blocks. The ball ricochets off Boddingtons, lands at Andechs Doppelbock Dunkel's boot, and is toe-poked goalwards from a stupid angle inside the six-yard box. Landlord is across in time — but the shot cannons off Pedigree, swerves wide of two more defenders, evades Harvey's and Old Peculier on consecutive bounces, and arrives — through what can only be described as fluid dynamics rather than football — at the right-hand post. Spaten Münchner Hell, waiting there for reasons known only to himself, taps it home. The 14th-century brewery has done it. Extra time.

Extra time

England were understandably deflated; Germany seemed too knackered to be elated. Fuller's London Pride gathered the eleven in the centre circle. He has captained this side through every round of the tournament. He had two things to say. The first was about cellar work. The second was about doing it for sixty years.

Whatever was said worked. England came out for extra time with their hands on the game.

101'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 3-2 GERMANY (Draught Bass) — OR DOES IT?!! From the centre circle, Fuller's London Pride threads a pass down the right for Adnams Ghost Ship, who is somehow still running. Ghost Ship arrives at the byline and cuts a low, looping ball back into the area. Bass collects it ten yards out, back to goal, brings it down with the outside of his boot, half-pivots, and lashes a swivelling shot off the underside of the bar. The ball drops vertically. It hits the line. It hits somewhere around the line — depending on which end you were drinking at. Brakspear, who has a tap-in if he wants it, has already turned away with both arms aloft, having decided the question for himself. The Germans surround the linesman. The linesman surrounds the Pub VAR booth. The Pub VAR booth consults its rulebook (still a beer mat). The verdict: the ball was in. Goal stands. The argument will outlive every brewer on the pitch.

Germany surrounded the linesman, the referee, the fourth official, the kit man, the man who delivers the beer to the ground, and the Pub VAR official's nan. None of it changed anything. The goal stood. They were going to lose this final the same way they had lost the last one in this country: arguing about a Brit's goal that came down off the bar.

Held — sorry, Erdinger — was the first to break clear afterwards, breaking down the inside left and shooting inches wide, a powerful shot that Landlord probably didn't have covered, but he'd handled the ball en route and play was pulled back. Hearts in mouths for England, then back in throats, then in glasses. The clock crept on. 110. 115. 119. And then.

120'GOAL!!! ENGLAND 4-2 GERMANY (Draught Bass) — HAT-TRICK!!! ENGLAND ARE THE BEER WORLD CHAMPIONS!!! Adnams Ghost Ship — who has somehow not stopped running — drills one last raking pass down the left channel. Bass is alone behind the German line. He moves like a 250-year-old beer being asked to do one more thing, which is to say: not quickly, but with the certainty of having done it before. A millisecond before Spaten Münchner Hell can close, Bass plants his standing foot and unloads the kind of shot that ends careers in the wrong direction. Top corner. He barely jogs the celebration; Brakspear sprints in to lift him off the ground. The Pub VAR commentator starts to say the thing everyone expects him to say, thinks better of it, and settles for: "That's it. That is very much it." ENGLAND, JUST AS PUB VAR SAID THEY WOULD, HAVE WON THE BEER WORLD CUP.

The Pub VAR pitch flooded with well-wishers. The German end stood for a long time, applauding. Augustiner shook hands with every Englishman on the field. Spaten dropped to his knees with the dignity of a brewery that has been doing this since 1397 and was not going to let one bad referee ruin a season. The Cask XI went down on the pitch as if they had been the underdogs all along. In a sense they had.

The trophy

The Pub Trophy was presented to Fuller's London Pride as captain. London Pride lifted it above his head with the slightly embarrassed expression of a man who had not really expected this. Draught Bass — who had not so much carried the team as carried the match ball home — stood at his shoulder with the kind of low, satisfied grin that 250-year-old Burton pale ales reserve for hat-tricks in finals. Timothy Taylor's Landlord stood on the other side grinning, having stopped more shots than any goalkeeper in tournament history. In conversation afterwards, Bass said only that he had been doing this since 1777 and that, sometimes, things took time.

Augustiner shook hands with the entire England squad with the dignity that comes from being the better team on the night and losing anyway. "Wir kommen zurück," he was heard to say into the Sky Sports microphone. We will come back. Of course they will. They have been coming back since 1328.

Notes from Pub VAR

Player of the match. Hat-trick in a final. The case for anyone other than Draught Bass ends at the sentence "the man scored three." The 18th-minute equaliser was the moment England settled into the game. The 101st-minute ghost goal was the moment England settled into history. The 120th-minute winner was the moment Germany ran out of seven hundred years. Bass takes the match ball home. He has been doing this for 250 years. It is, you suspect, the first match ball he has not had to share. Honourable mention to Timothy Taylor's Landlord, who kept five clean sheets in the previous six matches and only conceded twice in this one when whole brewing towns were sliding in around him. Goalkeeper of the tournament without question.

Manager of the tournament. The publican. Every publican. The cellar work that kept twelve different bitters in decent nick across a six-week tournament was the actual achievement here. Nobody hands out trophies for cellar work. They should.

Disappointment of the tournament. Belgium going out in the semis. Westvleteren had not been seen at a competitive event for years and the abbey had asked the committee, very politely, not to mention any of their players by name. The committee published the line-up anyway. The lesson, as ever, is that if you do not want to be talked about you should not be the second-best beer in the world.

Honourable mention. Czech Republic, who played the best lager football of any team and went home in the semi-finals only because Spaten remembered, in extra time, that it was Munich who actually finished the pilsner style and Plzeň had only started it. Pilsner Urquell took the defeat with the kind of grace that comes from inventing a style that the entire world now drinks.

Sixty years on

It is sixty years on from 1966. It is the same country hosting the World Cup that hosted Loaded's launch year in 1994. None of it was planned. All of it lined up anyway. The result was always going to be cask, because cask only exists properly through pubs, cellars, and place, and Britain is the only country with a national infrastructure willing to keep it.

Three ways to be old, three definitions of heritage, one trophy. Belgium have the oldest brewers. Germany have the oldest brewery. England have the oldest format. The trophy went where the format goes. Drink properly. Lift trophy. Roll credits.
