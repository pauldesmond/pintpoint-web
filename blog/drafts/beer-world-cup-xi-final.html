The Beer World Cup XI Final: Belgium 3-2 Germany

Beer World Cup XI · Final · 19 July 2026
🇧🇪 Belgium 3 – 2 Germany 🇩🇪
After extra time · Pub VAR confirmed · Westvleteren winner in the 110th
BelgiumRochefort 10 (33') · Orval (74') · Westvleteren 12 (110')
GermanyAugustiner Helles (12') · Spaten Münchner Hell (55')

The two great continental beer traditions have finally met in a final that mattered. Belgium's Trappist wall — captained by the world's most-hoarded beer, marshalled by three abbeys with a shared millennium of practice, finished by a wild-yeast idea nobody else in world brewing dares copy — has beaten Bavarian Reinheitsgebot 3-2 after extra time.

Germany did what Germany does: kicked off, kept possession, scored first, and looked, for large parts of the ninety, like a side that had been perfecting the same three things since 1516 and did not need a fourth. Belgium did what Belgium does: waited.

Westvleteren 12, whose brewery has for decades sold beer only from a hatch in the abbey wall on days it chose, walked forward in the 110th minute like a man who had never really believed he was here to play, and settled the game as if it were a question he'd been asked politely.

The Pub VAR booth confirms the score. Belgium 3, Germany 2, after extra time. The trophy goes to Flanders — and, technically, to a Cistercian order that would rather it didn't.

The road to the final

Belgium's route through the tournament was the tournament's quietest campaign. A group stage settled on the second matchday by an Orval winner nobody could quite defend; a round-of-sixteen tie against the United States that the Americans had wanted, badly, and lost 4-1 to the Trappist front six inside twenty minutes — Westvleteren 12, Rochefort 10 and Orval had walked the century wall down before the American hop shelf could get out of its own way. Belgium's quarter-final against Spain looked, on paper, like a fair fight — Alhambra Reserva 1925 and La Pirata Black Block have kept every European abbey side honest all tournament — and settled 2-1 on Rochefort's second-half header, an abbey specialty. The semi-final against Norway was the one everybody assumed would be the harder of Belgium's two. Nøgne Ø and Lervig Lucky Jack had the run of the middle third for forty minutes. Then Cantillon Fou' Foune came off the bench, opened a Belgian goal from a set piece that no coach on the pitch had ever seen executed before, and the abbey column closed the door. Belgium arrived at the final without anyone in the football press quite noticing they had.

Germany cruised through their side of the draw with the casual contempt of a side that has been brewing without interruption since the 14th century. Augustiner picked up two early group goals; Schneider Aventinus banged in a hat-trick against the Netherlands in the round of sixteen; Schlenkerla Märzen smoked his way past Mexico in the quarters — lager v lager, but one side had been doing it four centuries longer. The semi-final against USA could have gone either way until Spaten Münchner Hell stepped up in extra time and reminded everybody that the 1994 hosts had only learned to brew the style properly by importing the people who'd been doing it for seven hundred years.

Bavarian 4-4-2. Brewmaster: Reinheitsgebot. Tactical philosophy: do the same thing perfectly, forever.

Pre-match

By kick-off the omens had stopped being subtle. A German captain founded in 1328. A Belgian captain that the world's beer press had to promise not to name in the pre-match interview and then named anyway. Two beer traditions that had never really needed to prove they were beer traditions — one built on a purity law, one built on a rule of silence — meeting for the first time in a game where the outcome mattered.

The teams emerged from the tunnel to the kind of pandemonium you only get at a Pub VAR final. Germany in crisp Bavarian whites with black trim, doing those nervous little jogs that bottom-fermented lagers do when there is a referee somewhere. Belgium in monastic browns and cream — Westvleteren 12 ambling out with the yellow cap of the 12 balanced on his head, Rochefort 10 already looking like he was thinking about something more important than football, Orval doing the loose-limbed stretches of a beer that has three yeasts in it and no clear plan for any of them. Augustiner Helles and Westvleteren 12 shook hands at the coin toss like two men who had already agreed the game was largely ceremonial and one of them would win it anyway. The oldest beer on the pitch was, arguably, the recipe. File that away.

First half

Germany kicked off. They always do. Augustiner Helles took possession in the centre circle and held it for what felt like the entire first ten minutes — a beer that does not so much attack as occupy. Schneider Aventinus had a half-chance from a long ball played in by Weihenstephaner Hefeweißbier; Tegernseer Hell flashed a wide shot from the edge of the area that the Pub VAR review confirmed was definitely refreshing but not on target.

And then, twelve minutes in, the pressure told.

12'GOAL!!! BELGIUM 0-1 GERMANY (Augustiner Helles) — Weihenstephaner Hefeweißbier delivers a low cross from the left. Schlenkerla Märzen flicks it on. Augustiner Helles, almost unmarked at the back post, controls it on his chest and rolls it past the abbey line into the corner. Seven hundred years of practising the same finish does that. The German end roars. The Belgian end blesses itself and orders another Duvel, defensively. Eric Todd in the press box mutters something about lab coats and pipettes.

Belgium responded the way Belgium responds. Not quickly. They played twenty minutes of unhurried possession, four of them from Saison Dupont in midfield holding the ball as if the referee had never blown for kick-off. Chimay Bleue rattled a header off the bar. Duvel had a shot pushed round the post. And then, from a rehearsed corner nobody had noticed being rehearsed:

33'GOAL!!! BELGIUM 1-1 GERMANY (Rochefort 10) — Cantillon Fou' Foune, playing wider than any lambic normally would, drives a corner short to Saison Dupont. Dupont pauses, sells one, and clips it deep into the second six-yard-box zone. Rochefort 10 — 11.3% ABV, unbothered by air travel, unbothered by physics — meets it with the calm of a beer that has been brewed by the same monks since 1595 and hangs the header into the top corner. The German back four look at each other. The German back four have questions. The answers, four hundred and thirty years of them, are not forthcoming.

The rest of the half settled into hand-to-hand brewing. Spaten and Paulaner took control of the midfield, knocking the ball back and forth with the air of two breweries that have shared a Munich postcode for four hundred years. Erdinger blootered a header that St. Bernardus Abt 12 headed off the line. Schlenkerla Märzen smoked a free kick straight into the wall. The half ended 1-1. The Pub VAR booth checked the line three times on Rochefort's header. The line was fine. The line is always fine.

Second half

Germany came out re-pressed. Ayinger Celebrator was brought on for Tegernseer Hell at the break, a more direct doppelbock substitution that gave Germany more weight in the air. The pressure built. Belgium held it as they had held everything all tournament: patiently, with a rule about not talking during service. Then, ten minutes in, Germany broke through.

55'GOAL!!! BELGIUM 1-2 GERMANY (Spaten Münchner Hell) — A quick one-two between Andechs Doppelbock Dunkel and Paulaner Salvator carves open the Belgian midfield. Spaten Münchner Hell arrives late, unmarked, from the right — collects the ball on the edge of the D and drives a low finish across the abbey keeper into the far corner. The finish is 1397 vintage: no fuss, no celebration to speak of, just a slight bow to the crowd and back to the halfway line. The Bavarian end sings something about Wiesn. Germany, twelve minutes into the second half, have their nose back in front.

Belgium did not panic. Belgium does not do panic. They brought Boon Oude Geuze off in the 62nd and brought St. Bernardus Abt 12 up alongside Rochefort 10 as a second front-post specialist. The change looked, at first, defensive. It wasn't.

Pub VAR — 68th minuteErdinger Weißbier goes to ground under contact from Achel Bruin at the edge of the German box. On-field call: no penalty. Pub VAR consults the rulebook (still a beer mat with handwriting on it). Verdict: no penalty. Weißbiers of that pour tend to lose their heads at the end of a long second half. Play on.

Six minutes later, the equaliser. The kind of goal only Belgium can score, because only Belgium fields beers that behave like this.

74'GOAL!!! BELGIUM 2-2 GERMANY (Orval) — Duvel drives at Hofbräu Original down the left channel and slips the ball inside to Saison Dupont. Dupont — dry-hopped, farmhouse, allergic to anything predictable — chips a floated ball over the German back four toward the penalty spot. Orval, who runs on Brettanomyces and a second fermentation nobody outside the abbey fully understands, controls it with the outside of his boot, spins, and lashes an unrepeatable shot into the top left corner. The Bavarian keeper takes half a step, decides against the rest of the step, and just stands and watches. There is no scouting report for what Orval does. There has never been a scouting report for what Orval does. The Belgian end erupts. Orval crosses himself and jogs back to the halfway line.

Fifteen minutes to go. Belgium and Germany traded caution. Erdinger blazed a shot wide; Chimay Bleue headed just over from an Achel corner. The referee added three. The three ticked down without incident. Extra time. The Reinheitsgebot side looked, for the first time all tournament, tired.

Extra time

Belgium came out of the tunnel for extra time in silence. That was noticed. The abbey side had barely spoken a word all match; they barely spoke a word in the huddle. Westvleteren 12 pointed at the German half. Rochefort 10 nodded. That was the entire team talk. Cantillon Fou' Foune, characteristically, was already back on the pitch smelling the grass.

Germany started extra time the way they had started the match: possession, patience, a Bavarian side confident it would eventually be Bavarian again. But something had shifted. Belgium were pressing higher. Duvel had been switched to a false nine and was tucking in behind Weihenstephaner. And then, in the 110th minute, Westvleteren 12 finally, finally decided to walk forward.

110'GOAL!!! BELGIUM 3-2 GERMANY (Westvleteren 12) — WESTVLETEREN 12 HAS SCORED IN THE FINAL. Rochefort 10 wins a fifty-fifty with Andechs Doppelbock Dunkel deep in the German half. He rolls it wide to Orval, who — having already scored the strangest goal of the final — passes on the chance and slips a threaded ball back inside to Westvleteren 12 on the edge of the D. There is no run. There is no feint. There is a single touch to settle, a half-glance up, and a finish across the German keeper into the far bottom corner that is so unhurried it is almost rude. Westvleteren, who has spent his career actively not being available for purchase, has walked forward once and settled the World Cup final. He does not celebrate. He does not raise his arms. He nods at Rochefort 10, who nods back, and they both jog quietly back to the halfway line. A CISTERCIAN GOAL IN A WORLD CUP FINAL.

Germany rallied. Of course they rallied. Weihenstephaner Hefeweißbier headed just over from a Schlenkerla free kick with three minutes left. Erdinger drove one straight at St. Bernardus Abt 12 in the six-yard box. In the last minute of the last minute, Paulaner Salvator hit the post — a doppelbock rebounding off woodwork nine minutes into stoppage time is a sentence that can only occur in football, and even there rarely — and the ball rolled harmlessly out of play. The whistle went. The abbey held. Belgium had won the Beer World Cup.

The trophy

The Pub Trophy was presented to Westvleteren 12 as captain. Westvleteren lifted it above his head with the slightly embarrassed expression of a Trappist who had not, technically, been given permission to be here. Rochefort 10 stood at his shoulder, a beer so overqualified for this moment he was almost bored by it. Orval stood on the other side grinning, which was the first time all tournament anyone had seen Orval grin. Chimay Bleue kept looking at the ceiling as if checking that God had noticed. Duvel was already opening himself.

Augustiner shook hands with the entire Belgian squad with the dignity that comes from being the better side on the night, in some fair sense, and losing to a beer that had come out of an abbey hatch and gone home to it again. "Wir kommen zurück," he was heard to say into the Sky Sports microphone. We will come back. Of course they will. They have been coming back since 1328.

Westvleteren, asked for a comment, gave the standard Sint-Sixtus reply: "We would prefer not to be interviewed about this."

Notes from Pub VAR

Player of the match. A one-touch winner in a World Cup final, from a beer that spends most of its life refusing to be sold. Westvleteren 12 takes it. The 33rd-minute Rochefort header was the moment Belgium remembered they were Belgium; the 74th-minute Orval moment was the moment Germany realised they were playing a squad without a rulebook; the 110th-minute Westvleteren finish was the moment abbey brewing showed up in a football result. Honourable mention to Orval, whose 74th-minute equaliser is now on every pub whiteboard in Flanders and half of Wallonia, and whose Brettanomyces should probably be investigated by UEFA for enhanced fermentation.

Manager of the tournament. The abbots. Every abbot. The cellar work that kept eleven monastery beers in Champions League-level nick across a six-week tournament without commercial pressure to speak of was the actual achievement here. Nobody hands out trophies for silence. They should.

Disappointment of the tournament. England going out to Paraguay. The cask side had arrived talking a bigger game than they turned out to have — Draught Bass, Fuller's London Pride and Timothy Taylor's Landlord were fancied by most neutrals to reach at least the quarter-finals — and instead met a squad of Paraguayan Pilsen variations that turned out to be sharper, meaner, and more patient than the cask XI's third-round tap list could handle. There will be a longer piece on this. The short version is that warm and flat requires the pub to be nearby, and Paraguay had brought their own weather. English cask, in the end, does not travel.

Honourable mention. USA, who played the best craft-lager football of any team and went home in the semi-finals only because Spaten remembered, in extra time, that Munich had been brewing the style for seven hundred years before Brooklyn started over-pricing it. Brooklyn Lager took the defeat with the kind of grace that comes from being the youngest serious lager-brewing nation in the world and still going toe-to-toe with the oldest. The 1994 hosts return home with a record richer than the football would tell you.

Two ways of taking beer seriously

Germany takes beer seriously by never changing anything. The Reinheitsgebot is 510 years old, the recipes older, the breweries older still, and the argument for Bavarian brewing is: we have been doing this longer than most countries have existed and we have not stopped once. Belgium takes beer seriously by leaving the door open. Trappist certification requires only that a beer be brewed under monastic supervision, inside abbey walls, with any commercial surplus going to charity. What actually goes in the kettle is negotiable — hence Orval's Brettanomyces, hence Cantillon's spontaneous fermentation, hence Westvleteren's decision to sell only from a hatch.

Two definitions of continuity, one trophy. Germany have the oldest brewery; Belgium have the oldest excuse not to explain themselves. The trophy went where the excuse went. Drink properly. Lift trophy. Roll credits.
