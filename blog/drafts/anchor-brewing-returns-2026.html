Essay22 May 2026 · San Francisco · Industry

Anchor Steam is brewing again. The craft beer it created is a different thing now.

Production signals at Potrero Hill, label approvals for Steam and Old Foghorn, a billionaire owner. Resurrecting a foundation myth into a market that no longer needs the same kind of foundation.

For most of a year after Hamdi Ulukaya bought Anchor Brewing in May 2024, nothing happened. The Potrero Hill brewhouse stayed quiet, the Sapporo-era closure of July 2023 hardened into the new normal, and the only public sign of life was a billionaire's name on a deed. Anchor stories drifted toward eulogy. Then, this January, the steam came back.

Equipment work. Grain deliveries to the loading bay. Actual visible steam from the brewhouse stacks. In late April, federal label approvals (COLAs) landed for Anchor Steam Beer and Old Foghorn — not just the parent labels, but the working production paperwork a brewery needs to put liquid in cans. The reopening is no longer rumour. It's pre-production with paperwork.

What it's not, yet, is a date. Ulukaya has not announced one. The former union workforce — laid off in 2023, told repeatedly they'd be first in line for any revival — reports being given, in one of them's words, the cold shoulder on rehires. Veteran 21st Amendment co-founder Shaun O'Sullivan appears to be consulting. There's enough motion to be sure beer is coming. There isn't enough public information to know whose Anchor it'll be.

What Anchor was for

To understand why this matters more than another brewery reopening, you have to remember what Anchor was actually for. Not as a product — Steam was always good, never great by 2010s standards — but as a story. Fritz Maytag bought the brewery in 1965 from a near-bankrupt owner because the world's last Steam Beer brewery was about to disappear and Maytag thought that was, simply, intolerable. He had no business plan. The American craft brewing industry didn't exist; he was inventing it as he went.

Everything since — Sierra Nevada, Sam Adams, the late-80s microbrewery explosion, the early 2000s craft boom, the post-2010 hazy-IPA-and-pastry-stout generation — built downstream of Maytag's bet. Anchor wasn't the biggest brewery in any decade. It was the founding image. The independent, the tradition-keeper, the brewery that proved a small American operation could survive on character rather than scale. As Jeff Alworth wrote in 2023, "Anchor was never just a brewery. It was wrapped up in the myth of America."

When Sapporo closed it in 2023, the loss wasn't a beer going off the market. It was the founding myth going off the market. Alworth's piece described the feeling exactly: "the ground may suddenly feel spongy under our feet." Whatever craft beer was, it became a different thing the moment Anchor stopped.

Reopening into a different room

So what does it mean to bring it back?

The honest answer is: less than people will want it to mean. Anchor closed in 2023 into a craft market that was already past peak; it reopens in 2026 into one that is meaningfully smaller. US craft beer volume dropped 5% through 2025. The hazy IPA economy has cooled. Bars and bottle shops have less shelf space for legacy brands. Anchor's national distribution evaporated during the closure and would have to be rebuilt from scratch — a capital-heavy lift few mid-size revivals attempt.

The geography of taste has also shifted. Anchor Steam was always a category of one — a lager-ale hybrid fermented at California Common temperatures — but the modern American drinker has either moved to ultra-haze West Coast IPAs at the craft end or returned to mass-market lagers at the everyday end. Steam sits in a middle ground that fewer people are reaching for. The brewery's other historical strengths — Liberty Ale (one of the first dry-hopped American pales), Old Foghorn (the beer that defined modern American barleywine) — sit in categories that today's category leaders define rather than follow.

None of this means the reopening will fail. It does mean the reopening, even if successful, won't be the same Anchor. It can't be. The thing Maytag was for in 1965 — proving that small American brewing could survive — has been so thoroughly proven that the proof itself is no longer interesting.

What this actually tests

There are three real questions worth watching, in roughly this order.

One — does Ulukaya rehire the unionised workforce? The Chobani founder built his public reputation on labour-progressive politics. The 2023 Anchor workers, after a two-year fight, are the most directly affected constituency of any of his decisions. A revival without them would be a real story about what billionaire stewardship of American craft actually means in practice.

Two — what does the relaunch lineup say about the read on the market? The COLAs are for Steam and Old Foghorn. That's a heritage play: the two beers most embedded in Anchor mythology, neither of them a category leader today. A leaner Anchor that leans into being a heritage producer (Steam, Old Foghorn, maybe Christmas Ale) for a smaller, regional, loyal audience is one strategic answer. Trying to compete with the modern craft circuit — adding hazy IPAs, pastry stouts, the contemporary lineup — would be a different one. The label approvals so far suggest the former.

Three — does Anchor still mean what Anchor used to mean? A brewery is a story plus liquid. The liquid will be back relatively soon. The story is harder. Whatever Anchor reopens as in 2026 will be measured not against the 1965 founding myth but against the more anxious 2026 question: can American craft, with national volume declining and consolidation accelerating, support a revival of its own foundation story? The answer to that one matters beyond San Francisco.

What to do, as a drinker

If you have a chance to drink Anchor Steam again — buy it, share it. The brewery deserves it. The story deserves it. And specifically as a drinker who remembers what Steam tasted like in the 1990s versus the 2010s versus right before the closure: judge the beer on what it tastes like when it comes back, not on the cultural weight of the label. The mythology will help fund the relaunch. Whether the relaunch earns its place will be a different test.

Anchor was the proof that you could build an American brewery on character rather than scale. The interesting question now is whether, with the proof already absorbed into the foundations of every craft brewery that followed, character alone is still enough.
