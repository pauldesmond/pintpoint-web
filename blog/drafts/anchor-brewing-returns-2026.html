Anchor Brewing's coming back. The 2021 rebrand isn't.

Steam in the Potrero Hill stacks, federal label approvals, a billionaire owner — and a homepage in May 2026 still wearing the 1896 logo, not the abstract mark drinkers hated.

Visit anchorbrewing.com in May 2026 and what greets you is the varsity-block ANCHOR mark — rope-and-anchor detail, Since 1896, the identity Anchor wore for most of a century. Not the 2021 rebrand. Not the abstract yellow-and-blue mark that beer drinkers, beer writers, and design critics all watched land at the same time and didn't recognise as Anchor.

The rebrand was on the brand for roughly two years. The brewery closed in July 2023. Whether the rebrand caused the closure is an argument; that it didn't help it survive is closer to a fact. For plenty of drinkers it reads now like the final visible nail before the lid went on the box.

What's coming back is the older Anchor. The brewhouse stacks are showing steam again. Grain deliveries are reaching the loading bay. In late April, federal label approvals (COLAs) landed for Anchor Steam Beer and Old Foghorn — not just the parent labels, but the working production paperwork a brewery needs to put liquid in cans. Hamdi Ulukaya, the Chobani founder who bought Anchor in May 2024, has not announced a date. But the revival is no longer rumour. It's pre-production with paperwork.

What the rebrand was for

The 2021 rebrand was Sapporo's attempt to push Anchor's identity onto a generic modern shelf. Defector put it bluntly: a campaign to "blandify their logo for frictionless consumer identification." Out went the instantly recognisable old-timey anchor mark. In came what the trade design site Beer Branding Trends — which is, in principle, supportive of ambitious rebrand work — described as a "clunky" anchor illustration, "garish and unrefined" colours, typography needing "more refinement and consideration," and an overall execution that looked "half-baked and stock... like an early sketch."

The reaction was severe enough that Anchor published a public response trying to defend the work. Two years later, when the brewery closed, plenty of drinkers cited the rebrand as the moment Anchor stepped away from itself and never quite stepped back. Whether the rebrand was the wound or only the symptom, the timing is hard to ignore: 125 years of brand equity overwritten in 2021, two years of declining shelf presence, then a closure that the corporate parent communicated to long-serving staff via press release.

The revival, so far, isn't repeating that mistake. The varsity-block mark is what's facing the world from the homepage in May 2026. The 2021 mark isn't.

What Anchor was for

To understand why this matters more than another brewery reopening, you have to remember what Anchor was actually for. Not as a product — Steam was always good, never great by 2010s standards — but as a story. Fritz Maytag bought the brewery in 1965 from a near-bankrupt owner because the world's last Steam Beer brewery was about to disappear and Maytag thought that was, simply, intolerable. He had no business plan. The American craft brewing industry didn't exist; he was inventing it as he went.

Everything since — Sierra Nevada, Sam Adams, the late-80s microbrewery explosion, the early 2000s craft boom, the post-2010 hazy-IPA-and-pastry-stout generation — built downstream of Maytag's bet. Anchor wasn't the biggest brewery in any decade. It was the founding image. The independent. The tradition-keeper. The brewery that proved a small American operation could survive on character rather than scale. As Jeff Alworth wrote on closure day in 2023, "Anchor was never just a brewery. It was wrapped up in the myth of America."

When Sapporo closed it, the loss wasn't a beer going off the market. It was the founding image going off the market. Alworth described the feeling exactly: "the ground may suddenly feel spongy under our feet."

Reopening into a different room

So what does it mean to bring it back?

The honest answer is: less than people will want it to mean. Anchor closed in 2023 into a craft market that was already past peak; it reopens in 2026 into one that is meaningfully smaller. US craft volume was down again through 2025. The hazy IPA economy has cooled. Bars and bottle shops have narrowed shelf space for legacy brands. Anchor's national distribution evaporated during the closure and would have to be rebuilt from scratch — a capital-heavy lift few mid-size revivals attempt.

The geography of taste has also shifted. Anchor Steam was always a category of one — a lager-ale hybrid fermented at California Common temperatures — but the modern American drinker has either moved to West Coast haze at the craft end or returned to mass-market lagers at the everyday end. Steam sits in a middle ground fewer people are reaching for. The brewery's other heritage strengths — Liberty Ale (one of the first dry-hopped American pales), Old Foghorn (the beer that defined modern American barleywine) — sit in categories that today's category leaders define rather than follow.

The COLAs that landed in April are for Steam and Old Foghorn. That's a heritage play: the two beers most embedded in Anchor mythology, neither a category leader in 2026. A leaner Anchor that leans into being a heritage producer for a smaller, regional, loyal audience is one strategic answer. Trying to compete with the modern craft circuit — adding hazy IPAs, pastry stouts, a contemporary lineup — would be a different one. The label approvals suggest the former, and the visible commitment to the older logo on the website is consistent with it.

The labour question matters too, in roughly first place by importance. Ulukaya built his public reputation on labour-progressive politics; the former unionised workforce — Anchor was the first union craft brewery in America — was told repeatedly they'd be first in line for any revival. The signals out of San Francisco have, so far, been quieter than that promise suggested. Veteran 21st Amendment co-founder Shaun O'Sullivan appears to be consulting. There's enough motion to be sure beer is coming. There isn't yet enough public information to know whose Anchor it'll be.

Will it come back to UK taps?

For UK drinkers — the audience PINtPOINT actually serves — Anchor was always rare. A handful of beer-led free houses and US-import-focused bars carried Steam in the 1990s and 2000s; beyond that you mostly encountered Anchor as a label in beer writing, not a beer in a glass. Trade conversation in the 2010s leaned on a reputation that Steam "didn't travel well" — that the lager-and-ale hybrid character that gave it its identity didn't always survive a transatlantic supply chain in good shape.

Whether that reputation was deserved or whether it was just easier to keep Steam off a UK draught line than to fight its quirks, the practical result was the same. Anchor in the UK was always more story than presence. By the time the 2023 closure landed, it was already, for most UK drinkers, a brewery you'd heard of without often encountering.

Whether the revival rebuilds any UK shelf or draught presence isn't a question Ulukaya's team has answered publicly. There's no logistical reason it can't — Christmas Ale used to land in better UK bottle shops on annual rotations — but a brewery rebuilding its core American distribution first has a long list of priorities before British supply chains. If Anchor Steam shows up on a UK tap line at some point in 2026 or 2027, that will be its own kind of signal: the international craft trade voting on whether the founding myth still earns its place on a tap line in 2026.

It'll be visible the moment it happens. That's what tap-list watching is for.

A radically traditional choice

Anchor's defining instinct, when it has had to choose between its older identity and a contemporary one, has been to choose the older. After the 1906 San Francisco earthquake destroyed the original brewery, its owners could have rebuilt it for the new century — ice, refrigeration, the modern German lager model the rest of the country was already adopting. They didn't. They rebuilt the brewery looking back to the nineteenth century instead. Anchor's own brewery historian David Burkhart calls it "a radically traditional decision." It is the move that, more than any single beer, defines the company.

The 2021 rebrand was the break with that pattern. The decision now visible on the May 2026 homepage — to put the heritage mark back at the front door, not the abstract one — is the return to it. Whether deliberate or default, it is consistent with what Anchor has done every time it has had to choose.

Anchor was the proof that you could build an American brewery on character rather than scale. The interesting question in 2026 is whether, with the proof absorbed into the foundations of every craft brewery that followed, character alone is still enough. The brewery is putting its older identity in the shop window. The market will decide whether the older identity still sells.
