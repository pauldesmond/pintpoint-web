Under the Hood: How PINtDEXTER Picks Your Pint

An earlier essay made the argument: predict the pint, not the rating. The follow-up question is how, exactly. This is the engineering version — four layers, the per-beer DNA that drives them, and the Dutch pale "lager" that surfaced the most recent fix.

A recommender is only as good as the features
it has about the thing it's recommending.

What the first essay didn't say

The earlier piece was about the user side of the matrix. Sip-or-Skip, Head-to-Head, preferred styles — all of that is how PINtPOINT learns what a drinker likes. It said comparatively little about the other side: how each beer gets a shape the recommender can compare against.

That side matters more than the user side. A taste profile is useless if the beers themselves are unstructured strings. You can know with great precision that someone wants something bitter and tropical and full-bodied, and still fail to recommend the right pint if all you know about the pint is "Hazy IPA, 6.2%, Cloudwater."

The default answer, and why it doesn't work for beer

The dominant approach to recommendation in the last fifteen years is collaborative filtering: factor a user-by-item rating matrix into low-rank components, and use those components to predict missing ratings. It's the thing Netflix made famous. It's also the architecture under most published beer recommenders — David Smith's yhat R prototype in 2013, NYC Data Science's Ninkasi, Ethan Haley's Untappd work, all hybridised variants of the same idea.

It depends on something craft beer doesn't have: a dense rating matrix. The UK alone releases more than a hundred new beers a week. Most of them never accumulate the rating volume that collaborative filtering needs. Cold start hits twice — once for new users with no history, once for new beers with no community signal. The fallback in both cases is popularity, which is a ranked list of famous things rather than a recommendation.

There's a second cost rarely talked about: the rating treadmill. Models like this need users to keep rating items, and keep rating recommended items, to refine themselves. The product becomes a labelling job. Drinkers will tolerate that for a while; they won't tolerate it forever, especially when the value back is "the famous beer is famous."

The other half of the field, suddenly cheap

The alternative to collaborative filtering has always been content-based recommendation: score items by their own features, not by which other users liked them. The classic example is Pandora, which paid human musicologists to label every song across hundreds of features before the recommender ever saw them.

That model was historically prohibitive for catalogues the size of beer. Twelve thousand beers, hand-labelled across eight axes, is months of work and a budget no consumer app can justify. Nishant Kushwaha's 2023 prototype got partway there by mining BeerAdvocate user comments with an LLM and matching extracted attributes against the user's selected preferences — clever, but the features still came from how much the community had already written about a given beer. Cold start in disguise.

What changed in 2025–2026 is that LLMs got good enough — and cheap enough — to label the beer, not its discussion. Given a beer's name, brewery, declared style and ABV, a small model produces a structured 8-axis profile in about two seconds for around £0.0025. Twelve thousand beers cost roughly thirty quid to score end-to-end, and the result caches forever. Pandora's labelling problem became a script that runs overnight, with no community discourse required.

What goes into a beer's DNA

Each row in the catalogue gets the following inferred features, plus the ABV and declared style that came from the source data:

  • Hoppy — aroma and flavour from hops (citrus, pine, tropical, floral). Not bitterness.
  • Bitter — palate bite from boil hops; the lingering, drying side of hop chemistry.
  • Sweet — residual sugar, lactose, caramel malt, fruit sweetness.
  • Fruity — juicy, estery, tropical, stone-fruit. Whether the source is hops, yeast, or adjuncts.
  • Roast — malt character of any colour: biscuit and toffee at the pale end, coffee and cocoa at the dark end.
  • Tart — sour or acidic, from wild fermentation or fruit puree.
  • Body — light, medium, or full.
  • Fermentation family — ale, lager, mixed, wild, cider, or mead. The one that matters most for cross-style mistakes.

Six of those become the visual signature in the app — the TastePentagon that appears on a beer's detail card and in Head-to-Head matchups. It's the same data either way; the brand mark is what makes it portable.

The four layers, end to end

PINtDEXTER stacks four signals. Each answers a different question. Take any one out and recommendations get worse in a different direction.

  1. Preference capture — Sip-or-Skip binary swipes and Head-to-Head pair rounds. Forced choices, not free-form ratings. Output: a soft style profile after about ten interactions.
  2. Sliders (TUNeDEXTER) — the user can take direct control across 13 style categories when the inferred profile is wrong. The app never overrides explicit slider settings; preference learning is suggestion, not law.
  3. Per-beer DNA — the LLM-scored content features above. Independent of any user. Cached per-beer, re-runs only on schema change.
  4. Availability filter — the live tap list at venues near the user. The recommendation is always tied to a pint you can order tonight, not a beer name to screenshot.

Layers 1 and 2 are the user side. Layer 3 is the beer side. Layer 4 is the world side. The cross-product is what gets surfaced.

The Amstel case

Mid-May 2026 a quiet bug surfaced. A drinker whose profile pointed firmly at pale ales was being served Amstel as an 83% match. Amstel is a pale lager, not a pale ale. The mismatch should never have scored that high.

The trace took about an hour. Amstel had been ingested with style="Pale Ale" — the source data was wrong. PINtDEXTER's cross-family penalty existed and worked correctly, but it relied on a lookup from declared style to fermentation family. Style said "Pale Ale," lookup said "ale," and the penalty for serving a lager to an ale fan never fired. The LLM's own rationale for the beer's flavour profile already read "a mainstream European lager-leaning pale, clean and restrained despite 'Pale Ale' label." The system knew. The recommender wasn't asking.

The fix is what you'd expect: give the LLM an explicit family axis and use that value in preference to the style-derived one. The override path means the recommender stops trusting the declared style when the LLM disagrees — Amstel now scores correctly, and the same correction propagates to every other beer whose Untappd category drifted (there are a few hundred, scattered through cider-pretending-to-be-fruit-beer and lambic-pretending-to-be-Belgian-ale cases).

It's a small change that fixes a class of bug, which is the most flattering thing you can say about an engineering change.

Why this scales

The thing that makes content-based recommendation work for PINtPOINT specifically is the price of LLM feature extraction in 2026. The whole catalogue scores for less than a pub lunch. New beers added by the menu pipeline get scored within a few minutes of ingestion. The recommender has a usable shape for every beer the moment it appears, before any user has rated it.

The implication is asymmetric: a user-side cold-start problem is solved by ten taps on the first launch. A beer-side cold-start problem is solved by the catalogue pipeline before the user ever sees the beer. Either way, the experience the user gets is "the app already knows enough to be useful" — not "rate things until we have a signal."

What we're not claiming

Content-based recommenders aren't new. LLM-based feature extraction isn't new — Kushwaha applied that pattern to BeerAdvocate comments in 2023, and live consumer products in the same shape have begun to surface. The flavour axes themselves are descended from generations of beer-writing convention — Cornell, Protz, Mosher, the BJCP guidelines.

The contribution worth flagging is narrow: feature extraction from the beer's own metadata (name, brewery, declared style, ABV) rather than from community discussion. That removes one more layer of cold-start, because a beer doesn't need to be famous enough to have been written about before it has a recommendation shape. Combined with the four-layer pipeline above, and the venue-bound question the app actually answers — which pint, on which bar, tonight — that's the part that's worth shipping.

Frequently asked questions

What is content-based recommendation and why does PINtPOINT use it?

A content-based recommender scores items by their own features rather than by which other users liked them. For beer, the features are the 6-axis flavour DNA (hoppy, bitter, sweet, fruity, roast, tart) plus body, fermentation family, and ABV. PINtPOINT extracts these features per beer with a small LLM that reads the beer's name, brewery, declared style and ABV — meaning a brand-new release from a tiny brewery has a usable shape on day one, before any user has rated it.

Why not just use collaborative filtering like other beer recommenders?

Collaborative filtering needs a dense user-by-beer rating matrix to work. Beer catalogues are heavy-tailed: a few hundred famous beers collect most ratings, the long tail collects almost none. Cold-start is brutal both for new users and for new beers. A content-based recommender sidesteps that — the beer's features come from text the LLM can read, not from waiting for community ratings to accumulate.

What are PINtDEXTER's four layers?

Layer 1 captures preference via Sip-or-Skip binary swipes and Head-to-Head pair rounds. Layer 2 lets the user override with explicit sliders across 13 style categories (TUNeDEXTER). Layer 3 is the per-beer LLM-inferred DNA — the content features. Layer 4 is the availability filter that scores those features against the live tap list at venues near the user.

What is the fermentation-family override?

Two beers with similar flavour numbers can taste fundamentally different if one is an ale and one is a lager — yeast character, finish dryness, and clean-versus-estery profile diverge sharply across fermentation families. PINtDEXTER applies a 0.5x penalty to cross-family matches. The family was originally derived from the beer's declared style; that broke when a beer's style was wrong (Amstel labelled "Pale Ale" but actually a pale lager). The LLM now scores fermentation family directly per beer, overriding the style-derived value when they disagree.

How much does it cost to score the whole catalogue with an LLM?

Roughly £0.0025 per beer at current Haiku 4.5 pricing. PINtPOINT's catalogue of around 12,000 beers costs about £30 to score end-to-end, and the result is cached forever — re-scoring only fires when the schema changes or a row is deleted. That's a price point at which content-based recommendation stops being an academic alternative and becomes the obvious default.
