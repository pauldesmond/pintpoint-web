Primary source for the Firkin Universe. Posted to `rec.food.drink.beer` by Sean Desmond (formerly s...@st-and.ac.uk, University of St Andrews), Roger Burton-West and Sean Keane. Source URL: https://groups.google.com/g/rec.food.drink.beer/c/dN9jaW0nOwI

## Headline facts that refine the existing Universe

### Ownership timeline (sharper than what's in the article)

- 1979: Bruce opens Goose & Firkin, 47-48 Borough Road SE1, **converted from a shut-down Truman house**
- Bruce era: 1979–1988
- 1988: chain sold to Midsummer Leisure (later renamed European Leisure)
- 1990: sold to Stakis Leisure
- By 1995: owned by Taylor Walker, "a division of the brewing giant Carlsberg-Tetley"
- 1993: three new London pubs opened
- 1994: "many more around the country" opened

(So the article's "Midsummer Inns" should be **Midsummer Leisure** / European Leisure. And the chain was already Carlsberg-Tetley by 1995 via Taylor Walker — that ownership thread is older than our article implies.)

### Dogbolter — origin story (editorial gold)

> While brewing an Earthstopper (approx. 7.5%) in the cellar brewhouse, David Bruce heard the telephone ring, so he clambered upstairs in his wellies to answer it. The call was longer than expected and by the time he returned the brew was no longer strong enough to be the notorious Earthstopper. He decided to make the best of his mistake and announced in the bar that there was no Earthstopper but that a "new" beer was to be launched by the name of Dogbolter (5.6-6.0%).

The NAME came from family lore — Bruce's favourite uncle in Yorkshire brewed a similarly strong beer, friends slipped into a beck on the way home and shrieked so loudly their dogs bolted onto the moors. Bruce already had the name. The accident gave him the recipe.

Editorial value: Dogbolter is the **defining Firkin product**, still brewed today by Ramsgate Brewery. Its origin is an accident named after a family anecdote. This story belongs in the article.

### The period-correct Firkin brewing spec (1995)

- **Malt**: East Anglian malted barley (later "White Otter" malt per a 1995 editor's note)
- **Hops**: Kentish Fuggles and Goldings (later "only Fuggles" per the 1995 editor's note)
- **Water**: Burtonised with crystals at 80°C
- **Process**: hot liquor → mash tun (1.5hr) → copper boil with hops (1.5hr) → cool to 18°C → ferment 4–5 days → racked with sturgeon-bladder finings → 2 days mature
- **Output**: 1,500 pints per brew per pub

This is the period-correct hop list. Any modern revival using Citra/Mosaic now has a primary source against which it fails the heritage-authenticity test.

### The Firkin Crawl — original passport challenge

Sean Desmond did it personally:

> I did the crawl when the requirement was ten London pubs in a single day. My shirt has "The Firkin Ale Trail" on the front, and the pubs' addresses on the back.

The CURRENT article says 12 pubs / no time limit (which matches the 1990 leaflet). The 1995 version was **10 London pubs in a single day** — same name "Firkin Ale Trail", different rules. The challenge evolved.

Also nationwide (not just London) by 1995 — passports could be stamped at any of the 30+ Firkin sites by then.

### Beer storage (matters for the revivals piece)

> Dogbolter seems to suffer the most in pubs that don't use casks. This is a result of its centralised brewing and distribution, especially in London. House ales tend to be brewed closer to the pubs that serve them.

So even in 1995, Dogbolter was being centrally brewed and distributed — the cask format mattered for taste. This pre-figures the "modern Firkin Brewery" revival (Fox & Firkin → 7 other bars) by 30 years; the operational model was already centralised-with-spokes.

### The World Tour — a second challenge

> Almost all Firkin pubs offer another special drinking event for the more sedentary. If you can drink nine or ten bottles of beer from different countries in one day, you get a World Tour T-shirt, and "they'll call your ambulance for free!"

A separate passport challenge alongside the Crawl. The 1995 World Tour at the Flounder: Molson (Canada), Rolling Rock (USA), Sol (Mexico), Steinlager (NZ), Budweiser-Budvar (Czech), Newcastle Brown (UK), Löwenbräu Pils (Germany), Grolsch (Holland), Tooheys (Australia).

### The Firkin Club (1995-era)

- £5 lifetime membership
- 10% discount on T-shirts + merch
- Monthly newsletter
- Advance notice of outings, beer tastings, brewery tours
- Operated from the Master Brewer / Taylor Walker office, **77 Muswell Hill, London N10 3PH**, phone 0181 883-6431

The 77 Muswell Hill address may still be findable. Worth checking what's there now.

### Prices winter 1994-5

- Bitter £1.30+
- Best Bitter £1.45+
- Dogbolter £1.80–£1.85
- Mistlet'Ale (8.0% Christmas brew) £2.00
- T-shirt £6–£7
- Sweatshirt £12
- **Dogbolter home-brewing kit (40 pints) £6**

That last one is gold for the revivals piece — even in 1995 Firkin sold DIY Dogbolter home-brewing kits. The brewery actively enabled the DIY tier of revival.

## NEW pub-chain candidate: Hedgehog & Hogshead

This is the most editorially significant new lead in the doc.

> After selling the Firkin chain, David Bruce founded the Hedgehog & Hogshead pubs.

In 1995 the H&H estate was:
- **Hedgehog & Hogshead, 100 Goldstone Villas, Hove BN3 3RX** (phone 0273 733660)
  House ales: Brighton Breezy Bitter (4.3%)
- **Hedgehog & Hogshead, 163 University Road, Highfield, Southampton SO2 1TS** (phone 0703 581124)
  House ales: Belcher's Best Bitter (4.3%), Bob's Bootleg Bitter (5.2%)
  T-shirt: "Hogswill do anything for a pint of Belcher's, there Snout Better"
- **Water Rat, Marsh Benham (near Newbury), Berkshire** — non-brewing, also Bruce-owned

Beers: **Hogbolter (5.8%)** — sister to Dogbolter. **Prickletickler (7.3%)** + **Slay Bells (8.0%)** winter/Christmas brews.

Bruce's post-Firkin career arc:
1. 1979: Goose & Firkin opens
2. 1988: sells Firkin to Midsummer Leisure
3. 1989/90: founds Hedgehog & Hogshead
4. 1995 (per this doc): 3-pub H&H estate

What happened to H&H next is a separate research question. **This could become its own PINtPRESS piece + Universe page**: "Bruce's second act". Different from the Firkin story because Bruce ran it himself.

## Pre-1999 ex-Firkin pubs (the original ghosts — before the chain collapse)

By 1995 some Firkin sites were ALREADY closed/separated. These are pre-collapse ghosts:

1. **Flamingo & Firkin (Kingston-upon-Thames)** — closed as Firkin, acquired by Saxon Inns, renamed Flamingo Brewery Company. 88 London Road, Kingston-upon-Thames. House ales by 1995: Fairfield Bitter (3.4%), Royal Charter (4.2%), Coronation (5.8%).

2. **Fleece & Firkin (Bristol)** — separated from chain pre-1995; still open under same name as of 1995.

3. **Fuzzock & Firkin (London, 77 Castle Road NW1)** — est. 1987, closed pre-1995. **This is the same site as our FAT I dog-head Stop 1 (Tapping the Admiral).** So Fuzzock was a ghost BY 1995, ten years before the chain collapse. The building survived as a pub under a different name.

4. **Phantom & Firkin (London, 140 Balaam Street, Plaistow E13)** — est. 1987, closed pre-1995. **This is the same site as our FAT I dog-head Stop 5 (Barnardo's now)**. Phantom was a ghost BY 1995 too.

Editorial implication: **two of our five "post-1999 ghost stops" were actually 1987-era openings that closed by 1995** — they're 30+ years dead, not 25. The article and crawl-page descriptions of these should note that. The chain collapse killed many; some had already gone before.

1. The Truman-house origin of the Goose site (one sentence, big editorial detail)
2. **Midsummer Leisure** (not "Midsummer Inns") + Stakis (1990) + Taylor Walker / Carlsberg-Tetley by 1995 — refines the ownership timeline
