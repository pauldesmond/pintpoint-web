Beerology — The Anatomy of a Pint

DRAFT skeleton. Section sketches below; prose to be filled out as the worked-card data lands (see "The first reading" section, awaiting a real beer scored honestly with eight numbers and a one-line gut take). Pull-quote candidates and FAQ deferred until the body lands.

A better way to picture what a beer actually feels like to drink. Not a rating, not a flavour wheel — a reading system that sits between sensory map, personality test, field guide, useful tasting interface, and (let's be honest) lightly unhinged pseudo-scientific pub language. Built so you can decide between four taps in 800ms with a glance.

Where existing flavour vocabulary stops

[DRAFT] Four-tap scene at the bar. Untappd tells you what people gave a beer, not what it'll be in your hand. Flavour wheels tell you what category a beer belongs to. Radar charts (including PINtPOINT's own TastePentagon) tell you the magnitude of each sensory axis. Sommelier palate maps walk you through entry, mid-palate, finish, aftertaste — the closest existing framework. But none of them answer the actual decision question: what does this beer feel like to drink, entry through finish, in glanceable form? That's the gap Beerology fills.

The palate map is real. The tongue map isn't.

[DRAFT] Quick honest note. The tongue-zoning diagram from Edwardian textbooks (sweet at the tip, bitter at the back) is debunked — taste receptors are distributed across the whole tongue. What sommeliers and Cicerones actually use is the palate map: the temporal-spatial arc of a sip through the mouth — entry, mid-palate, finish, aftertaste. That IS a real framework that trained tasters reference. Beerology's substance is sound palate-mapping. The Victorian anatomical-poster aesthetic is the visual identity play, not the science: brass-rim chrome and sepia-paper styling. Anatomical-museum-print on the outside, palate-mapping on the inside.

The three-level reading

[DRAFT] Each Beerology card has three layers. They compose: the first is quantitative, the second is experiential, the third is editorial. None of them works on its own; together they answer the decision question.

A. Core shape — eight axes

The quantitative spine. Each beer scored 1–10 on:

  • Body — light to full
  • Bitterness — soft to sharp
  • Sweetness — dry to plush
  • Roast — absent to pronounced
  • Fruit — clean to expressive
  • Finish — short to lingering
  • Softness — crisp to rounded
  • Complexity — straightforward to layered

[DRAFT] Eight axes = octagon, not the five-axis TastePentagon that lives in the app's beer cards. Those are intentionally different scales — the pentagon is a brand-mark silhouette that has to read at thumbnail size; Beerology is a deeper reading that earns its complexity. Both use the same underlying data; both have a place. The pentagon answers "is this kind of beer mine"; Beerology answers "what's it like to drink".

B. Drinker cues — what it does to your evening

[DRAFT] The genuinely novel layer. Most beer-rating frameworks can't ask this question; the rating-out-of-5 mode is fundamentally about ranking, not character. Drinker cues are short phrases that describe what a beer does rather than what it contains:

  • first-pint friendly · contemplative · session-safe
  • meal-proof · roast-forward · soft landing
  • crisp finish · pub-classic · show-offy
  • fireside · all-nighter risk · better on the second

[DRAFT] Three or four per beer. Not exhaustive — a curation. The phrase that closes the question "what kind of pinting am I signing up for" is more useful than another decimal place on a sensory axis.

C. Beerology overlay — the anatomical-poster register

[DRAFT] The editorial wrapper. Half-clinical, half-playful. Anatomy labels that act as section headings or callouts on the visual card:

  • first-contact sweetness · mid-palate spread · finish grip
  • roast centre · hop edge · pubability
  • session drift · aftertaste echo

[DRAFT] These are the wink-vocabulary. Used sparingly — one or two per card maximum. The visual seriousness of the chrome (Victorian brass, sepia paper, anatomical cross-section styling) is what licenses the editorial-overlay register to be playful without tipping into wankery. The discipline rule: the joke layer must never compromise scannability. If a reader can't decide between two pints in 800ms because of the Beerology framing, the format has failed. Decision-helpful first, distinctive second.

What this isn't

[DRAFT] Three honest disclaimers before anyone overstates what's happening here:

  • Not new sensory science. The eight axes are descended from generations of beer-writing convention — Cornell, Protz, Mosher, BJCP. Beerology contributes a presentation register, not a research breakthrough.
  • Not a tongue-map revival. The tongue map is debunked. We know. The anatomical aesthetic is a deliberate Victorian-poster visual joke, never a truth claim.
  • Not displacing other flavour viz. Flavour wheels answer "what style"; the TastePentagon answers "is this my kind of thing"; Beerology answers "what does drinking it feel like". Three views, one underlying data layer, complementary by design.

[DRAFT] What Beerology is: experience legibility. Serious enough to trust, funny enough to remember.

Best-of-breed tasting intelligence,
dressed in lightly unhinged pseudo-scientific pub language.
