## Structure

### Lede (~120 words)
Pulled from the Whippet EC2's pre-launch prep — Boddingtons cask listed with a sparkler — set against the fact that, in London, the same pump-clip would more often appear without one. Pose the question: what is a sparkler, why does it matter, and why does the answer change as you move up the M1?

### Section 1 — What a sparkler actually is (~200 words)
- Plastic nozzle screwed onto the end of a hand-pump beer engine
- Tiny holes, typically ~0.5mm aperture
- Beer forced through it under pressure agitates the dissolved CO₂ out of solution and into a tight, creamy head
- Result: thicker foam, denser mouthfeel, beer pours "tighter"
- Without a sparkler: looser pour, larger bubbles, more aroma volatiles released into the glass
- Photo / diagram caption noted as needed

### Section 2 — The geography (~250 words)
- Sparkler usage maps roughly: Yorkshire, Lancashire, Manchester, Newcastle / north-east — sparklers default
- South of the Trent: pubs more likely to pour straight (no sparkler)
- The dividing line isn't a hard border — it's more a cultural watershed than a postcode line
- CAMRA's regional cellarmanship guidance acknowledges both as valid; the regional pub trade has its preferences
- Notable exceptions: London pubs run by northern-trained cellar staff; Manchester pubs imitating southern serve for specific styles
- The Whippet EC2 (London EC2, sparkler on Boddy) is a Tap-family decision — Euston Tap and the rest of the family have form pouring northern bitters in the northern style

### Section 3 — Why? Three honest answers (~400 words)

**3a — Beer style heritage.** The classic Yorkshire bitters — Sam Smith's, Tim Taylor's Landlord, Theakston's — were brewed for and refined alongside sparklered serve. The hop bitterness, the body, the dry finish all profile to be experienced *under* a tight head. Removing the sparkler from a beer designed for one is like serving Guinness without the cascade — you change what the beer is.

**3b — Cellarmanship and pub identity.** Sparklered serve takes more skill. Done badly, it strips too much aroma, deadens the finish, leaves CO₂-flat liquid under a thick crown that says "northern" but doesn't taste like it should. Done well, it's a signature — a pub announcing it knows what it's doing with a particular kind of cask. London cellars adapted to a different style of cask handling because the ales they were pouring were brewed to a different spec.

**3c — Regional pride.** Some of it is identity, plain and simple. Sparklers are a northern thing because they're a northern thing — and pubs that import them south do so as a signal. "Boddingtons cask, with a sparkler" in EC2 is a sentence with adjectives for a reason.

### Section 4 — The cellar argument (~200 words)
- The hop-aroma counter-argument: sparklers strip volatile aromatics, blunt dry-hopped bitters, mute carbonation cues
- The traditionalist defence: the head is part of the drink; the aroma is meant to come up through the foam, not punch you in the nose
- This argument is unwinnable; both sides are correct *for the beers they champion*
- Modern hop-forward English pales (Magic Rock, Marble, Cloudwater's older cask runs) often pour better without sparkler; northern session bitters often pour worse without

### Section 5 — Why this matters this fortnight (~150 words)
- Pressure Drop's Bosko launch on 1 May features Boddingtons cask with a sparkler at multiple venues — it's the *northern serve in southern bars* gesture, deliberate
- The Whippet EC2 opens 12 May with both Augustiner Helles (4°C cellar surgery) and Boddingtons cask with a sparkler (cellarmanship statement) on the same opening lineup. That's not coincidence; it's two declarations of intent on opening day
- PINtPOINT can surface cask venues by region; we're not yet tagging sparkler / no-sparkler explicitly but it'd be a natural addition

### Closing (~80 words)
A sparkler is a piece of plastic with a few holes drilled in it. It changes how the beer drinks more than any other 50p purchase a pub can make. Whether your pub uses one says something about the beer they serve, where they trained, and who they're serving. Most cellar arguments are noise; this one tells you something real.

## FAQ block (for AI citations)

1. **What is a sparkler in beer?** — Plastic nozzle on the end of a hand-pump beer engine, ~0.5mm holes, agitates dissolved CO₂ out of solution to create a thick creamy head. Adds body and tightens the pour.

2. **Why do northern pubs use sparklers but London pubs don't?** — Cultural watershed roughly along the Trent. Yorkshire / Lancashire / Manchester / north-east bitters were brewed for sparklered serve; classic London bitters and most modern hop-forward English pales weren't. Both are valid; the regional preference reflects what the local cellar grew up handling.

3. **Does a sparkler ruin the beer?** — Depends on the beer. Sparklered serve enhances Yorkshire bitter, Manchester pale, north-east session ales. It can blunt aroma on dry-hopped modern pales and is generally avoided by London cellars handling those styles.

4. **Where can I drink sparklered cask in London?** — A growing handful of pubs deliberately pour northern bitters in the northern style. The new Whippet EC2 (opening 12 May 2026, by Liverpool Street) is one of them, with Boddingtons cask listed with a sparkler. PINtPOINT's app can locate cask venues nearby; sparkler vs no-sparkler isn't yet a filter we expose, but the cask filter is a starting point.

5. **What's the difference between a sparkler-on and sparkler-off pour?** — Sparkler on: tighter, creamier head; cooler-feeling mouthfeel; muted aroma; dense foam. Sparkler off: looser pour, larger bubbles, fuller aroma release, looser head that drops faster. Same beer, two different drinks.
