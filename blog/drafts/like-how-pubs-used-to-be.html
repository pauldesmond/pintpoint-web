Note18 May 2026 · UK · Save Our Pubs follow-up · By Sophie Ro

"Like how pubs used to be."

The Telegraph's first National Pub Day landed on Saturday — Telegraph Ale on the bar of almost 300 pubs, five winning £5,000 drinks tabs, and across the country, more people in pubs than usual on a sunny Saturday in May. By Sunday morning the reports were in. Three landlords, three quotes, one campaign worth following.

The framing the Telegraph led with — pubs are folding at a rate of four a day under business rates, National Insurance and inflation — needs a counter-image to land politically. Saturday was the counter-image. The pubs the campaign is trying to keep open, full of people who chose to be there because a national paper asked them to.

Dorset: "It made our customers feel really important"

At the White Horse Inn, Chris Sargent has been the landlord for 25 years. He told the Telegraph the pub was "much busier" than a normal Saturday in both the morning and afternoon. "We had people coming in clutching their copies of the Telegraph, or saying they had read about it online." The detail that stuck for him wasn't the foot traffic. It was the effect on the regulars: "It made our customers feel really important and proud of their pub. They were chuffed."

That's the campaign's softer KPI. A pub isn't just a P&L; it's a status object for the people who drink in it. National coverage that says "this place matters" is doing morale work that no marketing budget pays for.

Lastingham, Yorkshire: two barrels gone

At the Blacksmiths Arms in Lastingham (North York Moors, population ~80), general manager Lucy Sellars said the pub got through two nine-gallon barrels of Telegraph Ale — that's 144 pints, in a single village pub, in a single day. "We had people come in who said they had never been in the pub before but had read about the campaign in The Telegraph and wanted to check it out."

The combination — regulars plus campaign-driven first-timers — is the rare upside of a national-paper-pub-day. Not just "the locals showed up". New people walked in, because a campaign told them this pub was worth visiting. Telegraph Ale wasn't the draw; the implied curation was.

Scone, Perthshire: capacity by noon, kids in the car park

Across the border at Brown and Blacks in Scone, manager Pamela Rumgay reported capacity by midday — "that doesn't usually happen". Local football side Scone Thistle FC had organised a sponsored walk that finished at the pub for a pint of Telegraph Ale. Families stayed until "half seven, eight-ish". The car park got closed off and the kids played football in it.

And then the line that summed up the day:

"It's like how pubs used to be."— Pamela Rumgay, Brown and Blacks, Scone

That's the headline the campaign deserves. Not "300 free pints poured" — anyone can buy a marketing event. "Like how pubs used to be" is the cultural memory a Saturday-pub-day was supposed to surface, and it surfaced, in a closed-off car park in Perthshire, while kids played football and parents finished their pints inside.

So what now?

The free-pint day was the warm-up; the campaign's actual ask is structural. The Telegraph is calling on the Government to reverse the planned business-rates increases and the National Insurance rise, and to cut the red tape that closes pubs faster than declining footfall ever did. Whether Rachel Reeves takes that ask seriously in the next Budget is the question this campaign was built to answer.

Until then: Saturday proved the cultural defence side of the equation. The pubs worth saving are the ones already doing the work — live music, sponsored walks ending at the bar, kids in the car park, regulars who feel important when a national paper writes about their local. The Telegraph paid for the pint. Saturday afternoon, in 300 pubs, paid for everything else.
